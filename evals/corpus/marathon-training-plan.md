# Marathon Training Plan

## Overview

A marathon is 42.195 kilometers (26.2 miles). Most first-time marathon training plans span 16-20 weeks and assume you can already run 5-10 km comfortably. If you can't run 5 km yet, spend 8-12 weeks building up with a couch-to-5K program before starting marathon-specific training.

## Training Principles

### Progressive Overload

Increase weekly mileage by no more than 10% per week. Sudden jumps in volume are the leading cause of running injuries. A typical beginner plan starts at 30-35 km/week and peaks at 55-65 km/week three weeks before race day.

### The 80/20 Rule

Approximately 80% of your weekly mileage should be at an easy, conversational pace — you should be able to hold a full conversation while running. The remaining 20% can include tempo runs, intervals, or race-pace work. Most beginners run their easy runs too fast, which increases injury risk and impairs recovery.

### Recovery

Rest days are when your body adapts and gets stronger. Never skip rest days. Sleep 7-9 hours per night — growth hormone release during deep sleep drives muscle repair. Consider a full rest day and an active recovery day (walking, swimming, yoga) each week.

## Weekly Structure

A typical week in the build phase (weeks 5-12) might look like:

| Day | Session | Purpose |
|-----|---------|---------|
| Monday | Rest | Recovery |
| Tuesday | 8 km easy | Aerobic base |
| Wednesday | 10 km with 4 km at tempo pace | Lactate threshold |
| Thursday | 6 km easy | Recovery run |
| Friday | Rest or cross-training | Recovery |
| Saturday | Long run (18-30 km, increasing weekly) | Endurance |
| Sunday | 8 km easy | Active recovery |

## Key Workouts

### Long Run

The cornerstone of marathon training. Start at 15-16 km and add 2-3 km each week, with a cutback week every fourth week (reduce by 20-30%). Peak long run should be 30-35 km, reached 3 weeks before the race. Run long runs 45-90 seconds per km slower than your target marathon pace.

### Tempo Run

Sustained effort at your lactate threshold pace — roughly the pace you could hold for one hour of racing (approximately your half-marathon pace). Typical tempo sessions: 4-8 km at tempo pace, sandwiched between warm-up and cool-down. These train your body to clear lactate more efficiently and are critical for marathon endurance.

### Interval Training

Shorter, faster repetitions (e.g., 6 x 800m at 5K pace with 400m jog recovery) that build VO2max — your body's maximum capacity to use oxygen. Schedule intervals once per week at most. They improve running economy and speed, but are the most taxing sessions and require extra recovery.

## Nutrition

### Daily Nutrition

Marathon training increases caloric needs by 300-600 calories per day depending on volume. Focus on complex carbohydrates (whole grains, sweet potatoes, oats), lean protein for muscle repair (1.4-1.7g per kg of body weight daily), and healthy fats. Stay hydrated — aim for pale yellow urine as a rough hydration indicator.

### Fueling Long Runs

For runs over 90 minutes, practice your race-day nutrition strategy. The body stores roughly 90 minutes of glycogen at marathon effort. Beyond that, you need to take in 30-60 grams of carbohydrates per hour from gels, chews, or sports drink. Practice this in training — never try anything new on race day.

### Carb Loading

In the 2-3 days before the marathon, increase carbohydrate intake to 8-10g per kg of body weight. This maximizes glycogen stores. Reduce fiber and fat to avoid gastrointestinal issues on race day.

## Tapering

The taper is a planned reduction in training volume in the 2-3 weeks before the race. Reduce weekly mileage by 20-25% each week while maintaining intensity. The taper allows your body to fully recover and arrive at the start line fresh. Many runners feel sluggish or anxious during the taper — this is normal and expected. Trust the training.

## Common Injuries

- **Runner's knee (patellofemoral pain)**: Pain around or behind the kneecap, especially going downhill or sitting for long periods. Often caused by weak glutes and hips. Strengthen with single-leg squats, clamshells, and hip bridges.
- **IT band syndrome**: Pain on the outer side of the knee. Caused by tightness and weakness in the hip abductors. Foam rolling helps symptoms; hip-strengthening exercises address the cause.
- **Plantar fasciitis**: Stabbing heel pain, worst with the first steps in the morning. Roll a frozen water bottle under the foot. Stretch calves and Achilles tendon daily. Ensure shoes have adequate support.
- **Shin splints (medial tibial stress syndrome)**: Pain along the inner shin bone. Often from increasing mileage too quickly or running on hard surfaces. Reduce volume, run on softer surfaces, and strengthen calves with eccentric heel drops.

## Race Day Strategy

### Pacing

Start conservatively — the first half should feel almost too easy. Aim for negative splits (second half slightly faster than the first). Most first-timers who crash in the final 10 km went out too fast. Use a GPS watch but also learn to run by perceived effort.

### The Wall

"Hitting the wall" (bonking) occurs around 30-35 km when glycogen stores deplete. Proper long-run training, fueling, and pacing minimize the risk. If you feel it coming, slow down, take in carbohydrates, and focus on running to the next aid station rather than the finish line.

### Mental Strategies

Break the race into smaller segments. Focus on the current kilometer, not the remaining distance. Have a mantra for when it gets hard. Remember that discomfort is temporary and you've done the training.
