# Coffee Brewing Methods

## Understanding Extraction

Coffee brewing is the process of extracting soluble compounds from roasted coffee grounds using water. The key variables are grind size, water temperature, brew time, and the ratio of coffee to water. Under-extraction produces sour, thin coffee; over-extraction produces bitter, astringent coffee. The sweet spot is typically 18-22% extraction yield.

### Water Temperature

Ideal brewing temperature is 90-96°C (195-205°F). Water that is too hot extracts bitter compounds too quickly. Water that is too cool under-extracts, producing a flat, sour cup. Boil the kettle and wait 30-60 seconds before pouring, or use a temperature-controlled gooseneck kettle.

### Coffee-to-Water Ratio

A standard starting ratio is 1:16 (1 gram of coffee per 16 grams of water). For a stronger cup, try 1:14. For lighter coffee, go to 1:17. Weigh both coffee and water with a kitchen scale — volume measurements are inconsistent because bean density varies between roasts.

## Pour-Over (V60, Chemex)

Pour-over produces a clean, bright cup that highlights the coffee's origin flavors. Grind medium-fine (like table salt). Rinse the paper filter with hot water to remove paper taste and preheat the dripper. Bloom the grounds with twice their weight in water for 30-45 seconds — this releases CO2 trapped during roasting and ensures even extraction. Then pour in slow, concentric circles, maintaining a steady water level.

A typical V60 brew takes 2:30-3:30 total. Chemex uses a thicker filter, producing an even cleaner cup but requiring a slightly coarser grind and longer brew time (3:30-4:30).

## French Press (Immersion Brewing)

French press is a full-immersion method: grounds steep in water for the entire brew time. Grind coarse (like raw sugar). Add coffee, pour all the water at once, stir gently, place the lid on without pressing, and wait 4 minutes. Press the plunger slowly and pour immediately — leaving coffee in the press continues extraction and turns it bitter.

French press produces a full-bodied, rich cup because the metal mesh filter allows oils and fine particles through. It's forgiving of technique but unforgiving of grind size — too fine and the cup will be muddy and over-extracted.

## Espresso

Espresso forces hot water through finely ground coffee at 9 bars of pressure, producing a concentrated 25-35ml shot in 25-30 seconds. The fine grind and high pressure extract oils that form the crema — the golden-brown foam on top.

### Dialing In

Small adjustments to grind size dramatically affect espresso. If the shot runs too fast (under 20 seconds), grind finer. If it chokes the machine (over 35 seconds), grind coarser. Target a 1:2 ratio (18g in, 36g out) as a starting point for most medium roasts.

### Milk-Based Drinks

- **Cappuccino**: Equal parts espresso, steamed milk, and foam (roughly 1:1:1)
- **Latte**: Espresso with more steamed milk and a thin layer of foam (1:3-4)
- **Flat white**: Double ristretto (shorter shot) with microfoam — velvety steamed milk with tiny, integrated bubbles

## AeroPress

The AeroPress combines immersion and pressure brewing. It's portable, fast (1-2 minutes total), and nearly unbreakable. Use medium-fine grind, water at 85-90°C, steep for 60-90 seconds, then press gently for 20-30 seconds. The paper filter produces a clean cup, or use a metal filter for more body.

The AeroPress Championship has produced hundreds of winning recipes — experimentation is encouraged. The inverted method (flipping the AeroPress upside-down during steeping) prevents early drip-through and gives more control over steep time.

## Cold Brew

Cold brew steeps coarse grounds in cold or room-temperature water for 12-24 hours. The low temperature extracts fewer acids and bitter compounds, producing a smooth, sweet concentrate. Dilute the concentrate 1:1 with water or milk. Cold brew has higher caffeine per volume than hot coffee due to the long extraction time and higher coffee-to-water ratio (typically 1:8 for concentrate).

Filter through a fine mesh strainer lined with cheesecloth or a paper filter. Refrigerate and consume within 1-2 weeks.

## Water Quality

Brewing water should contain 50-175 ppm total dissolved solids. Distilled water produces flat coffee because minerals assist extraction. Very hard water (high calcium and magnesium) over-extracts and can leave chalky deposits. If your tap water tastes good to drink, it will probably make good coffee. Otherwise, use filtered water or a mineral recipe (Third Wave Water packets).
