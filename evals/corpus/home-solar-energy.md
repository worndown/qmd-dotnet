# Home Solar Energy

## How Solar Panels Work

Photovoltaic (PV) solar panels convert sunlight into direct current (DC) electricity using semiconductor cells, typically made from crystalline silicon. When photons strike the silicon, they knock electrons free from atoms, creating an electric current. A standard residential panel produces 350-450 watts under Standard Test Conditions (STC: 1,000 W/m² irradiance, 25°C cell temperature). Most homes require 15-30 panels (6-12 kW system) to offset their annual electricity consumption.

### Panel Types

- **Monocrystalline**: Cut from a single silicon crystal. Highest efficiency (20-23%), best performance in low-light and high-temperature conditions. Most expensive per panel but most cost-effective per watt due to fewer panels needed.
- **Polycrystalline**: Cast from multiple silicon crystals. Slightly lower efficiency (17-20%), blue-tinted appearance. Cheaper per panel but requires more roof space.
- **Thin-film**: Deposited as thin layers on glass or flexible substrate. Lowest efficiency (12-15%) but lightweight and can be applied to curved surfaces. Rarely used in residential rooftop installations.

## System Components

### Inverters

Panels produce DC power, but homes run on alternating current (AC). Inverters convert DC to AC. There are three inverter architectures:

- **String inverter**: One central inverter for the entire array. Cheapest option but the whole string's output drops to the level of the weakest panel (shade on one panel reduces the entire string).
- **Power optimizers + string inverter** (SolarEdge): Optimizers on each panel perform maximum power point tracking (MPPT) individually, then feed DC to a central inverter. Mitigates shading losses.
- **Microinverters** (Enphase): A small inverter on each panel converts DC to AC at the panel level. Best performance under partial shading. Highest cost but simplest monitoring — each panel reports independently.

### Battery Storage

A battery system stores excess solar production for use after sunset or during grid outages. Lithium iron phosphate (LFP) batteries are the current residential standard — safer chemistry than NMC (nickel manganese cobalt), longer cycle life (6,000+ cycles at 80% depth of discharge), and tolerant of daily deep cycling.

Typical residential batteries (Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ Battery, BYD Battery-Box) provide 10-15 kWh of usable capacity. A 10 kWh battery powers essential loads (refrigerator, lights, router, phone charging) for 8-12 hours. Whole-home backup during an outage requires 20-40 kWh depending on HVAC and cooking needs.

### Monitoring Systems

Modern solar systems include monitoring portals (SolarEdge monitoring, Enphase Enlighten, Tesla app) that display real-time production, consumption, battery state of charge, and grid import/export. Panel-level monitoring helps identify underperforming panels — a sudden drop in one panel's output indicates shading, soiling, or hardware failure. Set alerts for production anomalies and check the dashboard monthly.

## Installation Considerations

### Roof Orientation and Tilt

In the Northern Hemisphere, south-facing roofs receive the most annual solar irradiance. East-facing panels produce more in the morning; west-facing panels produce more in the afternoon (often preferred with time-of-use electricity rates that charge more during evening peak hours). The optimal tilt angle approximately equals your latitude — a 40° roof in New York at 40°N latitude is nearly ideal. Flat roofs use tilt-mount racking to achieve the optimal angle.

### Shading Analysis

Even partial shading dramatically reduces solar production. A single shaded cell in a panel can cut that panel's output by 30-50% (with optimizers/microinverters) or reduce the entire string (with a string inverter). Use a shade analysis tool (Solar Pathfinder, Aurora Solar, Google Project Sunroof) to evaluate obstructions — trees, chimneys, neighboring buildings — across all seasons. Trees that don't shade the roof in summer may block low-angle winter sun.

## Net Metering and Economics

Net metering allows homeowners to export excess solar electricity to the grid and receive a credit on their utility bill. When the sun is shining and the home isn't consuming all the solar production, the excess flows backward through the meter. At night, the home draws from the grid and uses those credits. In a 1:1 net metering arrangement, each exported kWh offsets one consumed kWh at the full retail rate.

Net metering policies vary by state and utility. Some jurisdictions have reduced net metering compensation to wholesale rates or added grid access charges. Check your utility's current policy before sizing a system — oversizing with poor net metering yields diminishing returns.

### Return on Investment

A typical residential solar system costs $2.50-3.50 per watt installed before incentives. The federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) provides a 30% tax credit through 2032. State rebates and SRECs (Solar Renewable Energy Certificates) may further reduce costs. In most US markets, the payback period is 6-10 years, with 25-year savings of $20,000-60,000 depending on electricity rates and system size.

## Maintenance

Solar panels require minimal maintenance. Rain washes away most dust and pollen. In dry or dusty climates, spray panels with a garden hose (no soap or abrasives) once or twice a year. Snow slides off south-facing panels naturally in most cases — don't climb on the roof to clear snow. Inspect the system annually for cracked panels, loose wiring, or inverter error codes. Panels degrade approximately 0.3-0.5% per year — a 25-year-old system still produces 85-90% of its original capacity.
