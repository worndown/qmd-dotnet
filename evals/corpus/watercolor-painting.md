# Watercolor Painting Techniques

## Properties of Watercolor

Watercolor is a transparent medium — pigment is suspended in a water-soluble binder (gum arabic) and applied to paper. Unlike oil or acrylic paint, watercolor is transparent: the white of the paper shows through the paint, providing luminosity. There is no white watercolor paint in practice; white areas are preserved by leaving the paper unpainted.

This transparency means you work from light to dark — apply the lightest washes first and build up to the darkest values. Mistakes are difficult to correct because you cannot paint a lighter color over a darker one.

## Essential Supplies

- **Paper**: Use 300gsm (140lb) cold-pressed watercolor paper. Lighter paper buckles when wet. Cold-pressed has a slight texture that holds washes well. Hot-pressed paper is smooth and better for fine detail but less forgiving.
- **Brushes**: Round brushes in sizes 6, 10, and 14 cover most needs. A flat 1-inch brush is useful for large washes. Natural hair (kolinsky sable) holds more water and releases paint more evenly, but quality synthetic brushes work well.
- **Palette**: A white ceramic or plastic palette with wells for mixing. The white surface lets you judge color accurately.
- **Two water containers**: One for rinsing, one for clean water. Change them frequently.

## Wet-on-Wet Technique

Apply paint onto wet paper or a wet wash. The pigment spreads and blends softly, creating atmospheric effects — ideal for skies, fog, and soft backgrounds. The wetter the paper, the more the paint spreads. Control the amount of water to control the spread.

### Blooms and Backruns

If you add more water to a partially dry wash, it pushes pigment outward, creating cauliflower-shaped edges called blooms or backruns. These are usually unwanted accidents — they happen when you touch a drying wash with a wet brush. To avoid them, either work quickly while the paper is uniformly wet, or wait until it's completely dry before adding more paint.

## Wet-on-Dry Technique

Apply paint onto dry paper. Edges are crisp and controlled. This is the technique for details, sharp lines, and defined shapes. Each layer must be completely dry before applying the next, or the colors will muddy.

## Glazing

Glazing is the technique of layering thin, transparent washes over dry underlying layers. Each glaze modifies the color beneath it — a yellow glaze over blue creates green; a red glaze over yellow creates orange. Glazing produces rich, luminous color that is impossible to achieve by mixing on the palette, because light passes through each layer and reflects off the white paper.

Let each layer dry completely before glazing. Use a light touch — too much brushwork can lift the underlying layer. Staining pigments (phthalo blue, alizarin crimson) are better for glazing because they bond to the paper fibers and resist lifting.

## Color Theory for Watercolor

### Warm and Cool Colors

Every pigment has a warm or cool bias. Cadmium yellow is warm (leans orange); lemon yellow is cool (leans green). A limited palette of one warm and one cool version of each primary (red, yellow, blue) gives you the widest mixing range with the fewest paints.

### Complementary Mixing

Mixing complementary colors (red+green, blue+orange, yellow+purple) produces neutral grays and browns. This is how you create natural-looking shadows — never use black paint for shadows, which looks flat and lifeless. Instead, mix a dark from the shadow's complement: a purple-leaning mix of ultramarine blue and alizarin crimson makes a beautiful shadow for yellow-lit scenes.

### Color Harmony

Analogous colors (neighbors on the color wheel) create harmonious, cohesive paintings. A landscape painted primarily in blues, greens, and teals feels unified. Add a small accent of the complement (warm orange in the foliage) to create a focal point without disrupting the harmony.

## Composition in Watercolor

Many composition principles from photography apply directly: the rule of thirds for placing focal points, leading lines to guide the viewer through the painting, and the use of value contrast (light against dark) to create emphasis. In watercolor specifically, leaving areas of untouched white paper is a powerful compositional tool — it draws the eye and represents the brightest light in the scene.

## Common Mistakes

- **Overworking**: Watercolor is at its best when fresh and spontaneous. Every additional brushstroke risks muddying the color. Know when to stop.
- **Using too little water**: Dry, chalky applications lose the luminosity that makes watercolor unique. Keep your brush loaded.
- **Not enough value contrast**: A painting with all mid-tones looks flat. Ensure you have a full range from near-white to near-black.
- **Muddy colors**: Caused by mixing too many pigments (keep it to 2-3 per mix) or by glazing complementary colors without intention.
