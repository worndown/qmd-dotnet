# Indoor Plant Care Guide

## Light Requirements

Light is the single most important factor for indoor plant health. Most common houseplants fall into three categories:

- **Low light**: Pothos, snake plant (Sansevieria), ZZ plant, peace lily. Tolerate north-facing windows and interior rooms. "Low light" does not mean "no light" — all plants need some natural or grow light.
- **Medium light**: Monstera deliciosa, philodendrons, calathea, ferns. Bright indirect light from an east-facing window or a few feet from a south-facing window.
- **High light**: Succulents, cacti, fiddle-leaf fig, bird of paradise. Direct sun for 4-6 hours daily. South or west-facing windows.

Rotate your plants a quarter turn every week or two so growth stays even. Leggy, stretched-out stems reaching toward the window indicate insufficient light.

## Watering

Overwatering kills more houseplants than underwatering. The top inch of soil should dry out between waterings for most tropical plants. Succulents and cacti need to dry out completely.

### How to Water

Water thoroughly until it runs from the drainage holes, then empty the saucer after 15 minutes. Never let plants sit in standing water — this causes root rot. Bottom watering (placing the pot in a basin of water and letting the soil wick upward) ensures even saturation and avoids compacting the soil surface.

### Signs of Trouble

- **Yellowing lower leaves, mushy stems**: Overwatering. Check roots for rot (brown, mushy roots vs. healthy white ones). Repot in fresh soil if rot is present.
- **Brown, crispy leaf edges**: Underwatering or low humidity.
- **Wilting despite moist soil**: Root rot — the roots can no longer absorb water.

## Soil and Potting

Most houseplants need well-draining potting mix. A good all-purpose recipe is 60% peat or coco coir, 30% perlite, and 10% orchid bark. Succulents need even more drainage — 50% mineral (perlite, pumice, or coarse sand) mixed with 50% regular potting soil.

Always use pots with drainage holes. Decorative pots without drainage (cachepots) should be used as an outer sleeve — keep the plant in a nursery pot with drainage inside the cachepot.

### When to Repot

Repot when roots circle the bottom of the pot, grow out of the drainage holes, or when water runs straight through without being absorbed. Go up only one pot size (1-2 inches in diameter). Spring is the ideal time. Avoid repotting a stressed or recently purchased plant — let it acclimate for a few weeks first.

## Humidity

Most tropical houseplants come from humid environments and prefer 50-60% relative humidity. Average home humidity in winter can drop to 20-30%, causing brown leaf tips and edges.

Ways to increase humidity:
- Group plants together (transpiration raises local humidity)
- Place pots on a tray of pebbles with water (water level below the bottom of the pot)
- Use a humidifier in the room
- Misting is largely ineffective — the moisture evaporates within minutes

## Fertilizing

Feed actively growing plants (spring through early fall) with a balanced liquid fertilizer (e.g., 10-10-10 or 20-20-20) diluted to half the recommended strength, every 2-4 weeks. Stop fertilizing in winter when growth slows.

Over-fertilizing causes salt buildup in the soil, visible as white crusty deposits on the soil surface. If this happens, flush the soil thoroughly with plain water and reduce fertilizer frequency.

## Common Pests

- **Fungus gnats**: Tiny black flies hovering around the soil. Larvae live in moist topsoil. Let the soil dry out more between waterings. Use sticky traps and a layer of diatomaceous earth on the soil surface.
- **Spider mites**: Tiny dots on leaf undersides with fine webbing. Thrive in dry air. Spray with diluted neem oil or insecticidal soap weekly until gone.
- **Mealybugs**: White, cottony clusters on stems and leaf joints. Dab with rubbing alcohol on a cotton swab, or spray with neem oil.
- **Scale**: Brown or tan bumps on stems and leaves. Scrape off with a fingernail or old toothbrush, then treat with neem oil.

Inspect new plants before bringing them home and quarantine them for 2 weeks away from your existing collection.

## Propagation

Many houseplants are easy to propagate:
- **Stem cuttings** (pothos, philodendron, monstera): Cut below a node (the bump where leaves or aerial roots emerge), place in water or moist perlite until roots develop (2-4 weeks), then pot in soil.
- **Division** (snake plant, peace lily, calathea): Unpot the plant and gently separate at the root ball into two or more sections. Repot each section individually.
- **Leaf cuttings** (succulents, begonias): Place a healthy leaf on moist soil. New plantlets develop at the base over several weeks.
