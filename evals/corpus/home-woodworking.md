# Home Woodworking Basics

## Getting Started

Woodworking is one of the most rewarding hands-on hobbies — you can build anything from a cutting board to a bookshelf with relatively few tools. Start with simple projects that teach fundamental skills before investing in expensive equipment.

## Essential Hand Tools

You can accomplish a surprising amount with just hand tools:

- **Tape measure and combination square**: Accurate measurement and marking are the foundation of good joinery. The old saying "measure twice, cut once" exists for a reason.
- **Hand saw**: A Japanese pull saw (ryoba) cuts on the pull stroke, which is easier to control than a Western push saw. The crosscut side handles cuts across the grain; the rip side handles cuts along the grain.
- **Chisels**: A set of bevel-edge chisels (6mm, 12mm, 20mm, 25mm) covers most tasks. Keep them razor sharp — a sharp chisel is safer and more precise than a dull one.
- **Block plane**: A low-angle block plane smooths end grain, chamfers edges, and trims joints. It fits in one hand and is endlessly useful.
- **Clamps**: You can never have too many clamps. F-clamps and bar clamps for glue-ups; spring clamps for holding pieces while you work.
- **Mallet**: A wooden or dead-blow mallet for driving chisels without damaging the handle.

## Power Tool Essentials

If budget and space allow, these power tools dramatically increase efficiency:

- **Cordless drill/driver**: Drilling pilot holes and driving screws. Essential for almost every project.
- **Circular saw**: Straight cuts in plywood and lumber. With a straight-edge guide, it replaces a table saw for many tasks.
- **Random orbital sander**: Sanding by hand is tedious. A random orbital sander does in minutes what takes an hour by hand. Start at 120 grit, then 180, then 220.
- **Router**: Creates decorative edges, dadoes (grooves), and rabbets (step cuts). A trim router handles most home projects.
- **Table saw**: The centerpiece of a workshop for ripping boards to width. Demands respect — always use a blade guard, riving knife, and push stick.

## Joinery Techniques

### Butt Joint

The simplest joint: two flat surfaces glued and screwed (or nailed) together. Structurally weak on its own because long-grain-to-end-grain glue bonds are poor. Reinforced with screws, dowels, or pocket screws, it's perfectly adequate for utility shelving and basic frames.

### Pocket Screws

Pocket-hole joinery (using a Kreg jig or similar) drills an angled hole through one board and drives a screw into the adjoining piece. It's fast, strong enough for face frames and furniture, and requires minimal skill. Hide pocket holes on the back or underside of the piece.

### Dovetail Joint

The dovetail is the gold standard of hand-cut joinery. Interlocking pins and tails create a mechanical lock that resists pulling apart, plus a large glue surface. Dovetails are traditionally used for drawer boxes and chest corners. Cutting them by hand requires practice — mark carefully with a dovetail gauge, cut to the waste side of the line, and pare with a sharp chisel.

### Mortise and Tenon

A tenon (rectangular tongue) fits into a mortise (rectangular hole). This is the strongest frame joint and has been used in furniture and timber framing for thousands of years. Cut the mortise first (easier to trim the tenon to fit than vice versa). The tenon should be one-third the thickness of the stock.

### Dado and Rabbet

A dado is a flat-bottomed groove cut across the grain; a rabbet is an L-shaped step cut along an edge. Both are used for shelving — the shelf sits in the dado or rabbet, providing support along its full width. Cut them with a router and straight bit or on a table saw with a dado stack.

## Wood Selection

- **Softwoods** (pine, cedar, spruce): Inexpensive, easy to work, widely available at home centers. Pine dents easily and takes stain unevenly without a pre-stain conditioner.
- **Hardwoods** (oak, maple, walnut, cherry): More durable, take finish beautifully, but cost 3-5x more than softwood. Buy from a lumber yard, not a home center, for better selection and pricing.
- **Plywood**: Sheet goods (4x8 feet) made from thin layers glued with alternating grain. Baltic birch plywood is excellent for furniture and shop projects — stable, strong, and attractive edges.

### Moisture Content

Wood must be dried before use. Kiln-dried lumber has 6-8% moisture content and is stable for indoor furniture. Air-dried lumber may be 12-20% — it will shrink and warp if used indoors without further drying. Use a moisture meter to check before building.

## Finishing

### Sanding

Progress through grits: 120 for shaping, 180 for smoothing, 220 for final prep. Sand with the grain, never across it. Between coats of finish, lightly sand with 320 grit to provide tooth for the next coat.

### Common Finishes

- **Danish oil**: Penetrating oil-varnish blend. Easy to apply (wipe on, wait 15 minutes, wipe off), low sheen, enhances grain. Not very protective against water or heat.
- **Polyurethane**: Durable, water-resistant film finish. Oil-based poly has a warm amber tone; water-based poly dries clear. Apply thin coats with a brush or wipe-on applicator, sanding between coats.
- **Shellac**: Fast-drying natural finish from lac beetle secretions. Beautiful warm tone, easy to repair (new coats dissolve into old ones), but not resistant to water or alcohol.
- **Wax**: Soft sheen, pleasant feel. Often used as a final topcoat over shellac or oil. Minimal protection on its own.

## Safety

- Always wear safety glasses when cutting or routing
- Wear hearing protection with power tools
- Keep blades and bits sharp — dull tools require more force, increasing the chance of slipping
- Never reach over a spinning blade
- Remove loose clothing and jewelry before using any rotating machinery
