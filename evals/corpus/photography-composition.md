# Photography Composition Techniques

## The Rule of Thirds

The rule of thirds is the most widely taught composition guideline. Imagine dividing your frame into a 3x3 grid with two horizontal and two vertical lines. Place your subject along these lines or at their intersections — called power points — rather than dead center. This creates visual tension and guides the viewer's eye naturally through the image.

Most cameras and smartphones have a grid overlay option. Enable it and practice aligning horizons with the top or bottom third line, or placing a person's eyes at an upper intersection point.

## Leading Lines

Leading lines are elements within a scene that draw the viewer's eye toward the subject or deeper into the frame. Roads, fences, rivers, architectural edges, and even shadows can serve as leading lines. Diagonal lines add dynamism, while converging lines (like railroad tracks) create depth and perspective.

A common mistake is having leading lines that point out of the frame or toward an unimportant element. Always trace where the line leads before pressing the shutter.

## Negative Space

Negative space is the empty or uncluttered area surrounding your subject. Rather than filling every corner of the frame, intentionally leaving large areas of sky, water, or solid color can emphasize the subject through isolation. Negative space conveys mood — openness, solitude, simplicity — and gives the viewer's eye a place to rest.

Minimalist photography relies heavily on negative space. A lone tree against an overcast sky or a single boat on a calm lake are classic examples.

## Depth and Layers

Photographs are two-dimensional, but you can create the illusion of depth by including a foreground, midground, and background. A landscape shot might use wildflowers in the foreground, a lake in the midground, and mountains in the background. Each layer adds visual interest and pulls the viewer into the scene.

Shooting at wider apertures (lower f-numbers) creates shallow depth of field, blurring the background and making the subject pop. Conversely, narrow apertures (higher f-numbers like f/11 or f/16) keep everything sharp from front to back — useful for landscapes.

## Framing Within the Frame

Use natural or architectural elements to frame your subject: doorways, arches, tree branches, or windows. This creates a frame-within-a-frame effect that directs attention inward and adds contextual depth.

## Symmetry and Patterns

Symmetry creates a sense of harmony and order. Reflections in water, architectural facades, and tiled floors are fertile ground for symmetrical compositions. Breaking the pattern — a single red umbrella among black ones — draws attention through contrast.

## Color Theory in Composition

Complementary colors (opposites on the color wheel, like blue and orange) create vibrant contrast. Analogous colors (neighbors on the wheel, like green, teal, and blue) produce harmonious, calming images. Use color deliberately: a warm subject against a cool background naturally separates figure from ground.

## The Golden Ratio

The golden ratio (approximately 1.618:1) appears throughout nature and classical art. The golden spiral — derived from this ratio — can guide placement of key elements along its curve. While harder to apply on the fly than the rule of thirds, it tends to produce compositions that feel naturally balanced.
