# Basic Sewing and Mending

## Essential Supplies

A basic sewing kit contains everything needed for most repairs and simple projects:

- **Hand needles**: A variety pack with sizes 5-10 covers most needs. Sharps (general purpose), betweens (short, for quilting), and embroidery needles (large eye for thicker thread).
- **Thread**: All-purpose polyester thread in black, white, and navy covers most repairs. Match thread color to the fabric, not the existing stitching. Polyester is stronger and more weather-resistant than cotton thread.
- **Fabric scissors**: Sharp 8-inch shears dedicated to fabric only — cutting paper dulls fabric scissors quickly. Small embroidery scissors (4-inch) for snipping thread and detailed work.
- **Pins and pincushion**: Straight pins with glass or ball heads for holding fabric layers together before sewing.
- **Seam ripper**: A small hooked blade for removing stitches. Essential for fixing mistakes and opening seams for repairs.
- **Measuring tape**: A flexible 150cm (60-inch) tape for body measurements and fabric.
- **Iron**: Pressing seams flat with an iron is critical for professional-looking results. A pressed seam lies flat; an unpressed one bunches and puckers.

## Hand Stitches

### Running Stitch

The most basic stitch: pass the needle in and out of the fabric at regular intervals (3-5mm). Used for basting (temporary stitching to hold fabric in place before permanent stitching), gathering, and simple seams that don't bear heavy stress. For speed, load multiple stitches onto the needle before pulling through.

### Backstitch

The strongest hand stitch, closest to a machine stitch in appearance and durability. Bring the needle up one stitch length ahead, then insert it back at the end of the previous stitch, creating an overlapping, continuous line. Used for seams that need to hold under stress — crotch seams, armholes, bag straps. On the back side, stitches overlap, doubling the thread at every point.

### Slip Stitch (Ladder Stitch)

An invisible stitch for closing openings from the outside — hemming, closing stuffed items, or repairing split seams. Work alternating small bites (2-3mm) from each side of the opening, pulling the thread taut every few stitches to draw the edges together. When done correctly, the thread is completely hidden between the folded fabric edges.

### Blanket Stitch

A decorative and functional edge stitch. The thread loops around the fabric edge, creating a series of connected L-shapes. Used to finish raw edges on felt and wool (preventing fraying), attach applique patches, and decorate blanket edges. Space stitches evenly at 5-8mm intervals.

## Common Repairs

### Replacing a Button

The most frequent clothing repair. Select a replacement button the same size and style as the original. Thread a needle with doubled thread (60cm total), knot the end. Position the button, sew through each hole 4-6 times. For shirts, create a thread shank: hold a toothpick between the button and fabric while sewing. This gap allows the button to sit above the buttonhole fabric without distortion. Finish by winding the thread around the shank several times and knotting on the underside.

### Fixing a Fallen Hem

Turn the garment inside out and examine the hem. If the original stitching has partially failed, remove the loose section and press the fold line flat with an iron. Re-sew using a slip stitch (invisible from the outside) or blind hem stitch, picking up only 1-2 threads of the outer fabric with each stitch. For a quick fix, iron-on hemming tape (fusible web) bonds fabric layers with heat — not permanent enough for frequently washed garments but fine for emergency repairs.

### Patching a Hole

For small holes (under 1cm) in knit fabrics, use a darning technique: weave a grid of thread across the hole, alternating over and under the cross-threads to recreate the fabric structure. Use thread that matches the garment weight.

For larger holes or woven fabrics, apply a patch. Cut a piece of matching or coordinating fabric 2cm larger than the hole on all sides. Pin the patch behind the hole, fold the raw edges of the hole under, and stitch around the opening with a slip stitch or topstitch. For decorative patches (visible mending), use contrasting fabric or sashiko-style running stitches.

### Repairing a Split Seam

Turn the garment inside out and align the seam edges. Pin in place. Backstitch along the original seam line, overlapping the intact stitching by 1cm on each end to prevent further unraveling. Knot and trim. For areas under heavy stress (underarm seam, inseam), reinforce by sewing a second line of stitching 3mm from the first.

## Sewing Machine Basics

A sewing machine dramatically increases speed and stitch quality. The straight stitch and zigzag stitch handle 90% of home sewing needs.

### Threading and Tension

Thread the machine following the numbered guides (every machine marks the path). The top thread travels through the tension discs, take-up lever, and needle. The bobbin provides the bottom thread. Correct tension means the two threads interlock in the middle of the fabric — if loops appear on one side, the tension on the opposite side needs tightening.

### Common Machine Stitches

- **Straight stitch**: The default for seams. Use 2.5mm stitch length for woven fabrics, 3mm for knits (slight stretch tolerance).
- **Zigzag stitch**: Stretches with the fabric — essential for knit hems and seams. Also used to finish raw edges (set to a wide, close zigzag).
- **Buttonhole stitch**: An automated or 4-step stitch that sews a buttonhole to the size of your button. Mark the position with chalk and the machine does the rest.

## Fabric Basics

Understanding fabric types prevents repair failures:

- **Woven fabrics** (cotton broadcloth, denim, linen, silk): Threads cross at right angles. Cut edges fray. Stable, don't stretch (except on the bias). Use straight stitch with appropriate needle size.
- **Knit fabrics** (jersey, rib knit, French terry): Interlocking loops that stretch. Use a ballpoint needle (rounded tip slides between loops instead of piercing them) and zigzag or stretch stitch to maintain elasticity.
- **Denim and canvas**: Heavy woven fabrics. Use a jeans needle (size 16-18) and heavy-duty thread. Walk the machine slowly through multiple layers; use the hand wheel near seam intersections where fabric is very thick.
