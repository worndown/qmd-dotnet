# Dog Training Basics

## Training Philosophy

Modern dog training is based on positive reinforcement — rewarding desired behaviors rather than punishing unwanted ones. Punishment-based methods (choke chains, shock collars, dominance theory) are outdated, scientifically discredited, and damage the trust between dog and handler. Positive reinforcement produces faster learning, better retention, and a happier, more confident dog.

### How Dogs Learn

Dogs learn through operant conditioning: behaviors that produce pleasant outcomes are repeated; behaviors that produce no reward or remove a pleasant thing are reduced. The timing of the reward is critical — you have approximately 1-2 seconds after the behavior to deliver the reward for the dog to make the connection. This is why clicker training or a verbal marker ("yes!") is so effective: the click precisely marks the instant the dog performs the desired behavior, even if the treat arrives a few seconds later.

## Essential Commands

### Sit

Hold a treat above the dog's nose and slowly move it backward over the head. The dog's rear naturally lowers as it follows the treat. The instant the bottom touches the ground, click (or say "yes!") and deliver the treat. Name the behavior ("sit") only after the dog performs it reliably with the lure — adding the word too early teaches the dog that the word is meaningless.

### Stay

Ask the dog to sit. Hold your palm out (stop signal) and wait one second. Click and treat. Gradually increase duration (3 seconds, 5 seconds, 10 seconds). Then add distance (one step back, two steps back). Then add distractions. Only increase one variable at a time — distance, duration, or distraction, never all three.

### Recall (Come)

The most important safety command. Start in a low-distraction environment. Say the dog's name plus "come" in an excited voice. When the dog arrives, throw a party — the best treats, enthusiastic praise. Never call the dog to you for something unpleasant (bath, nail trimming). Never punish a dog that comes to you, even if it took 5 minutes. You want "come" to mean "the best thing ever happens when I go to my human."

### Leave It

Hold a treat in a closed fist. The dog will sniff, lick, and paw at your hand. Wait. The instant the dog pulls away or looks at your face, click and give a different, better treat from your other hand. The lesson: ignoring the temptation pays better than going after it.

### Loose-Leash Walking

Dogs pull because pulling works — it gets them where they want to go. Stop walking the instant the leash goes tight. Stand still like a tree. When the dog looks back or creates slack in the leash, mark and reward, then resume walking. This is tedious and slow at first but teaches the dog that pulling stops forward progress.

A front-clip harness redirects pulling force to the side, making it mechanically harder for the dog to drag you. It's a management tool, not a training solution — pair it with the stop-and-wait method.

## Socialization

The critical socialization window is 3-14 weeks of age. During this period, safely expose the puppy to as many new experiences as possible: different people (men, women, children, people in hats, people with beards), surfaces (grass, gravel, metal grates), sounds (traffic, thunder recordings, vacuum cleaners), and other vaccinated dogs.

Positive experiences during this window build a confident, resilient adult dog. Lack of socialization is the number one cause of fear-based aggression later in life.

## Crate Training

A crate is a management tool, not a punishment. Properly introduced, most dogs view the crate as a safe den. Feed meals in the crate, toss treats inside randomly, and leave the door open so the dog enters voluntarily. Only close the door once the dog is comfortable going in and out freely. Never use the crate as punishment — "go to your crate!" shouted in anger creates a negative association.

Puppies can hold their bladder roughly one hour per month of age (a 3-month-old can last about 3 hours). Never crate a puppy longer than it can physically hold its bladder.

## House Training

Take the puppy outside immediately after waking, after eating, after playing, and every 1-2 hours otherwise. Go to the same spot each time. Wait patiently. The instant the puppy eliminates outside, mark and reward lavishly. If you catch the puppy having an accident inside, interrupt with a neutral "oops" and immediately carry it outside. If you find an accident after the fact, clean it up silently with an enzymatic cleaner — punishing a dog for something that happened minutes ago is meaningless; the dog cannot connect your anger to the past event.

## Behavioral Problems

### Jumping on People

Dogs jump because greeting face-to-face is natural canine behavior, and because humans inadvertently reward it with attention (even negative attention is attention). The fix: turn away and ignore the dog when it jumps. The instant all four paws are on the floor, mark and reward. Ask guests to do the same. Consistency is critical — if jumping works 1 out of 10 times, the dog will keep trying.

### Separation Anxiety

True separation anxiety — destructive behavior, howling, house soiling only when alone — requires gradual desensitization. Start with absences of 1 second (step out the door, step back in). Slowly increase duration over weeks. Leave a frozen Kong (stuffed with peanut butter and kibble) as a positive association with departures. Severe cases may benefit from veterinary consultation and anti-anxiety medication alongside training.

### Resource Guarding

A dog that growls or snaps when you approach its food, toy, or sleeping spot is resource guarding. Do not punish the growl — the growl is a warning, and punishing it teaches the dog to bite without warning. Instead, practice trading: approach the dog and drop something better next to the guarded item. Over time, the dog learns that your approach predicts something wonderful, not a loss.
