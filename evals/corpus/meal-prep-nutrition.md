# Meal Prep and Nutrition

## Macronutrients Explained

The three macronutrients — protein, carbohydrates, and fat — provide the calories your body needs. Each serves distinct physiological roles, and the ratio you consume shapes body composition, energy levels, and athletic performance.

### Protein

Protein provides amino acids for muscle repair, enzyme production, and immune function. Each gram supplies 4 calories. Active individuals targeting muscle maintenance or growth should consume 1.6-2.2 grams per kilogram of body weight daily. Spread protein intake across 3-5 meals for optimal muscle protein synthesis — the anabolic response plateaus around 0.4g/kg per meal (roughly 30-40g for most people).

High-quality protein sources include chicken breast (31g per 100g), eggs (13g per 100g), Greek yogurt (10g per 100g), tofu (17g per 100g), and lentils (9g per 100g cooked). Complete proteins (animal sources, soy, quinoa) contain all essential amino acids; plant sources can be combined throughout the day to cover all amino acids.

### Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred fuel for high-intensity activity. Each gram supplies 4 calories. They are stored as glycogen in muscles and liver — roughly 400-500g total in a trained individual. Complex carbohydrates (oats, brown rice, sweet potatoes, whole-grain bread) provide sustained energy with fiber and micronutrients. Simple carbohydrates (fruit, honey, white rice) digest quickly and are useful around workouts for rapid glycogen replenishment.

### Fat

Dietary fat is essential for hormone production (testosterone, estrogen), vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K are fat-soluble), and cell membrane integrity. Each gram supplies 9 calories. Minimum intake should be 0.5-1g per kilogram of body weight. Prioritize unsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish) and limit saturated fat to under 10% of total calories. Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils) should be eliminated entirely.

## Calorie Targets

Energy balance determines weight change: consume more calories than you burn (surplus) to gain weight, or fewer (deficit) to lose weight. A moderate surplus of 250-500 calories per day supports muscle gain with minimal fat accumulation. A moderate deficit of 500 calories per day produces roughly 0.5kg (1lb) of fat loss per week while preserving muscle, provided protein is sufficient and training continues.

Estimate your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) using a calculator based on the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, then adjust based on the scale trend over 2-3 weeks. Body weight fluctuates 1-2kg daily from water retention, sodium intake, and bowel contents — track the 7-day moving average, not daily weigh-ins.

## Batch Cooking Fundamentals

Meal prep saves time, reduces decision fatigue, and makes hitting calorie and macro targets trivially easy. The core strategy is batch cooking 2-3 protein sources, 2-3 carb sources, and several vegetables on one day (typically Sunday), then assembling into portioned containers for the week.

### Protein Prep

- **Chicken breast**: Season, bake at 200°C (400°F) for 22-25 minutes until internal temperature reaches 74°C (165°F). Slice into portions. Keeps 4 days refrigerated.
- **Ground turkey/beef**: Brown in a large skillet, season with taco or Italian seasoning. Versatile — goes into wraps, bowls, pasta, or salads.
- **Hard-boiled eggs**: Boil for 10-12 minutes, ice bath, peel. Ready-to-eat protein snack. Keeps 5 days refrigerated.

### Carb Prep

- **Rice**: Cook a large batch in a rice cooker. White rice stores and reheats better than brown. Cool and refrigerate within 1 hour to prevent bacterial growth.
- **Sweet potatoes**: Cube, toss with olive oil, roast at 220°C (425°F) for 25-30 minutes.
- **Overnight oats**: Combine rolled oats, milk, yogurt, and chia seeds in jars. Refrigerate overnight. Add fruit and honey in the morning.

### Vegetable Prep

Roast sheet pans of broccoli, bell peppers, and zucchini at 220°C for 15-20 minutes. Steam green beans and snap peas. Wash and chop salad greens, store in containers lined with paper towels to absorb moisture and extend freshness to 5-6 days.

## Meal Timing for Athletes

### Pre-Workout Nutrition

Eat a meal with carbohydrates and moderate protein 2-3 hours before training. A snack 30-60 minutes before (banana, rice cake with honey) tops off glycogen without causing GI distress. Fat and fiber slow digestion — minimize them close to training.

### Post-Workout Nutrition

The post-workout anabolic window is wider than often claimed — consuming protein within 2 hours of training is sufficient. A meal with 30-40g protein and fast-digesting carbs (white rice, fruit, or a protein shake with dextrose) accelerates glycogen resynthesis and starts muscle repair. Post-workout nutrition is most critical when training twice daily or when the next session is within 8 hours.

## Food Safety

- **Temperature danger zone**: Bacteria multiply rapidly between 4-60°C (40-140°F). Refrigerate cooked food within 2 hours (1 hour if ambient temperature exceeds 32°C).
- **Reheating**: Reheat to an internal temperature of 74°C (165°F). Microwave reheating is uneven — stir halfway and check multiple spots.
- **Storage limits**: Most prepped meals last 3-5 days refrigerated. Freeze portions you won't eat within 4 days. Label containers with the prep date.
