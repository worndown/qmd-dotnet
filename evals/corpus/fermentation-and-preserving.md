# Fermentation and Food Preserving

## What Is Fermentation

Fermentation is a metabolic process in which microorganisms — bacteria, yeast, or molds — convert sugars and starches into acids, gases, or alcohol. Humans have used fermentation for thousands of years to preserve food, enhance flavor, and increase nutritional value. Fermented foods contain probiotics (live beneficial bacteria) that support gut health and digestion.

## Lacto-Fermentation

Lacto-fermentation relies on Lactobacillus bacteria, which are naturally present on the surface of all fruits and vegetables. These bacteria convert sugars into lactic acid, which preserves the food and gives it a tangy flavor. The key requirement is an anaerobic (oxygen-free) environment and sufficient salt to suppress harmful bacteria while allowing Lactobacillus to thrive.

### Sauerkraut

Shred cabbage, mix with 2% salt by weight (20g salt per 1kg cabbage), and pack tightly into a jar or crock until the liquid released covers the cabbage. Weigh down the cabbage to keep it submerged. Ferment at 18-22°C for 1-4 weeks, tasting periodically. The longer it ferments, the more sour and complex the flavor. Refrigerate when it reaches your preferred level of sourness — cold temperatures slow fermentation nearly to a halt.

### Kimchi

Kimchi follows the same lacto-fermentation principle as sauerkraut but adds garlic, ginger, Korean red pepper flakes (gochugaru), fish sauce, and other vegetables (radish, scallion, carrot). Salt the Napa cabbage at 3-5% by weight and let it wilt for 2-4 hours before draining. Mix with the paste, pack into jars, and ferment at room temperature for 2-5 days before refrigerating.

### Fermented Hot Sauce

Blend fresh chili peppers with 3-5% salt by weight. Pack into a jar with an airlock lid (or burp a regular lid daily to release CO2). Ferment for 1-4 weeks. Blend again with vinegar to desired consistency. The fermentation mellows the raw heat and develops depth of flavor that vinegar-only hot sauces lack.

## Vinegar Fermentation

Vinegar is a two-stage fermentation: first, yeast converts sugar to alcohol (as in wine or cider); then Acetobacter bacteria convert the alcohol to acetic acid. To make apple cider vinegar, press apples, allow the juice to ferment with wild yeast for 2-3 weeks, then leave it exposed to air (covered with cheesecloth) for 4-8 weeks while Acetobacter forms a SCOBY (symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast) on the surface — this is the vinegar "mother."

## Kombucha

Kombucha is fermented sweet tea. Brew strong black or green tea, dissolve sugar (80-100g per liter), cool to room temperature, and add a SCOBY with starter liquid from a previous batch. Cover with a cloth and ferment for 7-14 days at 20-28°C. The SCOBY consumes sugar and produces a slightly fizzy, tart beverage with a small amount of alcohol (typically under 0.5% if fermented correctly).

For carbonation, bottle the kombucha with a small amount of fruit juice or sugar for a second fermentation of 2-4 days in sealed bottles. Use pressure-rated bottles — CO2 buildup can shatter thin glass.

## Yogurt and Kefir

Yogurt fermentation uses thermophilic (heat-loving) bacteria — primarily Lactobacillus bulgaricus and Streptococcus thermophilus. Heat milk to 82°C to denature whey proteins (this thickens the yogurt), cool to 43°C, add a spoonful of existing yogurt as a starter, and hold at 43°C for 6-12 hours in an oven with just the light on or a dedicated yogurt maker.

Kefir uses mesophilic cultures in the form of kefir grains — gelatinous clusters of bacteria and yeast. Add grains to room-temperature milk, leave for 24 hours, strain, and drink. Kefir grains are reusable indefinitely and produce a wider variety of probiotic strains than yogurt.

## pH and Safety

The pH scale measures acidity. Most harmful bacteria (including Clostridium botulinum, which causes botulism) cannot survive below pH 4.6. Proper lacto-fermentation typically reaches pH 3.5-4.0 within the first few days. Use pH test strips to verify, especially when trying new recipes. If a ferment smells foul (not just sour), develops pink or black mold, or is slimy in a way you didn't expect, discard it.

White film (kahm yeast) on the surface is harmless but can impart off-flavors. Skim it off and ensure the vegetables remain submerged below the brine.

## Equipment

- **Fermentation crocks** with water-sealed lids allow CO2 to escape while preventing air from entering
- **Airlock lids** for mason jars serve the same purpose at a lower cost
- **Kitchen scale** for accurate salt measurements — eyeballing salt is the most common cause of failed ferments
- **pH strips or meter** for safety verification
- **Fermentation weights** (glass or ceramic) to keep vegetables submerged
