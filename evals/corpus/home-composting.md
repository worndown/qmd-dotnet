# Home Composting

## Why Compost

Composting converts organic waste into nutrient-rich humus — dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling material that improves soil structure, water retention, and microbial life. It diverts 30-40% of household waste from landfills, where organic matter decomposes anaerobically and produces methane, a potent greenhouse gas. Finished compost provides slow-release nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium plus trace minerals, reducing or eliminating the need for synthetic fertilizers.

## The Carbon-to-Nitrogen Ratio

Composting is a microbial process that requires the right balance of carbon-rich materials (browns) and nitrogen-rich materials (greens). The ideal carbon-to-nitrogen (C:N) ratio is 25-30:1.

### Brown Materials (High Carbon)

- Dry leaves (C:N ~60:1)
- Cardboard and paper (C:N ~350:1) — shred into small pieces
- Straw and hay (C:N ~80:1)
- Sawdust from untreated wood (C:N ~400:1) — use sparingly; very high carbon ties up nitrogen
- Wood chips (C:N ~500:1) — best used as mulch rather than in active compost
- Dryer lint from natural fiber clothing

### Green Materials (High Nitrogen)

- Fruit and vegetable scraps (C:N ~15:1)
- Coffee grounds and tea leaves (C:N ~20:1)
- Fresh grass clippings (C:N ~20:1) — add in thin layers; thick clumps mat together and go anaerobic
- Garden weeds (before they go to seed)
- Eggshells (slow to decompose but add calcium)
- Manure from herbivores (horse, rabbit, chicken — C:N ~15-25:1)

### Materials to Avoid

Never compost meat, fish, dairy, or oily food waste in an open bin — they attract rodents and produce foul odors. Avoid pet waste (dog, cat) due to pathogens. Don't add diseased plants or weeds that have gone to seed (unless your compost reaches thermophilic temperatures). Avoid treated or painted wood and glossy printed paper.

## Composting Methods

### Hot Composting (Thermophilic)

Hot composting is the fastest method, producing finished compost in 4-8 weeks. The key is building a pile large enough (at least 1 cubic meter) to generate and retain heat. Layer greens and browns, moisten to the consistency of a wrung-out sponge, and turn the pile every 3-5 days to introduce oxygen.

A properly managed hot pile reaches 55-65°C (131-149°F) in the core within 2-4 days. These temperatures kill weed seeds, plant pathogens, and fly larvae. Monitor temperature with a long-stemmed compost thermometer. When the temperature drops after turning and no longer rebounds, the active phase is complete. Cure the compost for 2-4 weeks before use to allow final decomposition and stabilization.

### Cold Composting (Passive)

Cold composting requires minimal effort: add materials as they become available and wait 6-18 months. The pile doesn't reach thermophilic temperatures, so weed seeds and pathogens may survive. It's suitable for gardeners with small, steady streams of organic waste who don't need compost on a schedule.

### Vermicomposting (Worm Composting)

Vermicomposting uses red wiggler worms (Eisenia fetida) to process food waste into worm castings — an exceptionally nutrient-rich amendment. A worm bin can be kept indoors (garage, basement, under the kitchen sink) and processes 1-2 kg of food waste per week per 1,000 worms.

Set up a bin with shredded newspaper or cardboard bedding, moistened to sponge consistency. Add worms and bury food scraps in the bedding. Worms eat the bedding and food, excreting castings. Avoid citrus, onions, and spicy foods — the acidity and strong compounds repel worms. Harvest castings every 2-3 months by pushing finished material to one side and adding fresh bedding and food to the other; worms will migrate toward the food over 1-2 weeks.

## Troubleshooting

### Pile Smells Bad

A foul, ammonia-like smell indicates too much nitrogen (excess greens) or anaerobic conditions (too wet, too compacted). Add brown materials to absorb excess moisture, turn the pile to reintroduce oxygen, and mix in dry carbon sources (shredded cardboard, dry leaves). A properly balanced compost pile should smell earthy, like a forest floor.

### Pile Not Heating Up

If a hot compost pile fails to reach thermophilic temperatures: it may be too small (below the 1 m³ threshold), too dry (add water while turning), lacking nitrogen (add greens), or lacking oxygen (turn more frequently). Check the C:N ratio and pile size first — these are the most common culprits.

### Fruit Flies and Pests

Bury food scraps under 10-15 cm of brown material. Keep a layer of dry leaves or straw on top of the pile at all times. For worm bins, freeze food scraps before adding to kill fly eggs. If rodents are an issue, switch to a fully enclosed tumbler composter or hardware-cloth-lined bin.

## Using Finished Compost

Finished compost is dark brown to black, crumbly, with no recognizable food or plant material remaining. It should smell pleasant and earthy. Mix it into garden beds at a ratio of 2-5 cm worked into the top 15 cm of soil. Use it as top dressing for lawns, potting mix amendment (25-30% compost by volume), or seed-starting medium when sifted through a 6mm screen. Compost tea — compost steeped in aerated water for 24-48 hours — can be applied as a foliar spray or soil drench to introduce beneficial microbes directly to plant roots.
