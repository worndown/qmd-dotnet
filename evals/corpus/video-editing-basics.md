# Video Editing Fundamentals

## The Editing Workflow

Video editing transforms raw footage into a coherent story. The basic workflow is: import footage, organize clips, assemble a rough cut, refine the edit, add transitions and effects, color grade, mix audio, and export the final file.

### Organizing Footage

Before cutting a single frame, organize your project. Create bins (folders) for different types of media: A-roll (main footage), B-roll (supplementary shots), audio, graphics, and music. Label every clip with a descriptive name. On large projects, use color coding or star ratings to flag selects (the best takes).

## The Cut

### Hard Cut

The most common edit: one shot ends and the next begins immediately. A hard cut is invisible when it follows the viewer's attention — cutting on action (a door opening, a hand reaching) makes the transition feel natural because the viewer's brain is focused on the movement, not the edit.

### J-Cut and L-Cut

In a J-cut, the audio from the next scene starts before the video transitions — the viewer hears a conversation before seeing the speakers. In an L-cut, the audio from the current scene continues over the next shot. Both techniques smooth transitions and create a sense of continuity. Dialogue scenes in films use J- and L-cuts constantly.

### Jump Cut

A jump cut is an abrupt cut between two shots of the same subject from a similar angle, creating a jarring skip forward in time. Jump cuts were once considered errors but are now a deliberate stylistic choice in YouTube videos and documentaries to condense monologues or show the passage of time.

### Match Cut

A match cut transitions between two shots that share a visual element — shape, color, movement, or composition. A classic example: a spinning coin cuts to a spinning planet. Match cuts create thematic connections between scenes.

## Pacing and Rhythm

The length of each shot controls the pace. Fast cuts (1-3 seconds) create energy and tension — action sequences, music videos. Longer shots (5-15 seconds) give the viewer time to absorb information — documentaries, slow-burning dramas. Vary the rhythm: a sequence of quick cuts followed by a long, steady shot creates a sense of release.

Cut on the beat of the music when editing to a soundtrack. Even in dialogue-driven content, maintaining an internal rhythm — matching cut points to speech patterns or natural pauses — produces a more engaging edit.

## Audio Editing

Audio is half the viewing experience. Poor audio (hiss, echo, uneven levels) makes a video feel amateur more than any visual imperfection.

### Levels and Mixing

- **Dialogue**: -12 to -6 dB (the loudest element)
- **Music**: -18 to -12 dB (fills space without competing with dialogue)
- **Sound effects**: Variable, but generally below dialogue

Use keyframes to automate volume changes — ducking the music under dialogue and bringing it back up during pauses. Monitor with headphones, not speakers, to catch imperfections.

### Removing Background Noise

Most editors include noise reduction tools. Sample a section of "room tone" (audio with no speaking) and use it as a noise profile to subtract the consistent hiss or hum. Apply noise reduction conservatively — aggressive settings make dialogue sound hollow and robotic.

## Color Grading

Color grading adjusts the color and tone of footage for aesthetic or narrative purposes. The process has two stages:

1. **Color correction**: Make the footage look natural and consistent. Adjust white balance, exposure, and contrast so that skin tones are accurate and shots from different cameras or times match each other.

2. **Creative grading**: Apply a deliberate look. Teal-and-orange grading is popular in cinema (warm skin tones against cool backgrounds). Desaturated, high-contrast grades convey grit or tension. Warm, saturated grades feel nostalgic or inviting.

Use scopes (waveform, vectorscope, histogram) rather than trusting your eyes alone — monitor calibration and ambient room lighting affect color perception.

## Framing and Reframing

If you shot in 4K but are delivering in 1080p, you have room to reframe — digitally zooming or repositioning the shot in post without losing resolution. This can rescue poorly framed shots, create push-in effects, or simulate a second camera angle from a single-camera shoot.

Follow the rule of thirds when reframing: place the subject's eyes at an upper third intersection. Leave more space on the side the subject is looking toward (lead room).

## Export Settings

- **YouTube / web**: H.264 codec, 1080p or 4K, 8-15 Mbps bitrate, AAC audio at 320kbps
- **Archive / master**: ProRes 422 or DNxHR — minimal compression, large files, suitable for future re-editing
- **Social media (vertical)**: 1080x1920 (9:16 aspect ratio), H.264, 5-10 Mbps

Always keep your project files and raw footage — rendered exports are lossy and cannot be re-edited to the same quality.
