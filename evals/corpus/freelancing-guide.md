# Freelancing and Self-Employment Guide

## Getting Started

Freelancing means selling your skills directly to clients rather than working as a full-time employee. Common freelance fields include software development, graphic design, writing, consulting, photography, and video production. The appeal is flexibility and autonomy; the challenge is inconsistent income, self-discipline, and handling your own business operations.

### Legal Structure

In most countries, you need to register as a sole proprietor or form a single-member LLC. An LLC provides liability protection — if a client sues, they can go after the business assets but not your personal savings or home. Consult a local accountant or attorney to choose the right structure for your jurisdiction.

## Finding Clients

### Platforms

- **Upwork, Fiverr, Toptal**: Marketplace platforms that connect freelancers with clients. Useful for building a portfolio but take 10-20% of your earnings. Competition is fierce, and a race to the bottom on pricing is common.
- **LinkedIn**: Publish articles and case studies in your area of expertise. Inbound leads from LinkedIn tend to be higher-quality and higher-budget than marketplace clients.
- **Personal website**: A simple portfolio site with 3-5 case studies, a clear description of your services, and a contact form. This is your credibility anchor — clients who find you through referrals will check your site before reaching out.

### Referrals and Networking

After your first few projects, referrals become the primary growth engine. Deliver exceptional work, meet deadlines, and communicate proactively — happy clients tell other potential clients. Attend industry events, join online communities, and offer genuine help before asking for business.

## Pricing

### Hourly vs. Project-Based

Hourly billing is simple but penalizes efficiency — the faster you work, the less you earn. Project-based pricing (a fixed fee for a defined scope) rewards expertise and speed. Estimate the hours, multiply by your target hourly rate, then add a 15-20% buffer for scope creep and communication overhead.

### Setting Your Rate

Research market rates for your skill and experience level. As a starting point, take the annual salary you'd earn as an employee, divide by 1,000 (this roughly accounts for taxes, benefits, and non-billable time). A $80,000 salary becomes an $80/hour freelance rate. Adjust upward for specialized skills or tight timelines.

### Value-Based Pricing

For experienced freelancers, price based on the value delivered, not the hours spent. If your marketing strategy generates $500,000 in revenue for a client, charging $25,000 for the project is reasonable regardless of whether it took 40 hours or 100.

## Contracts and Scope

Never start work without a written contract. At minimum, include:
- **Scope of work**: Exactly what you will deliver (and what you won't)
- **Timeline**: Milestones and deadlines
- **Payment terms**: Amount, schedule (50% upfront / 50% on delivery is standard), and late payment penalties
- **Revision policy**: How many rounds of revisions are included
- **Intellectual property**: Who owns the work product upon final payment
- **Termination clause**: How either party can end the engagement

Scope creep — the client gradually adding "just one more thing" — is the biggest profit killer in freelancing. The contract is your protection.

## Taxes and Finances

### Self-Employment Tax

Freelancers pay both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare taxes (in the US, roughly 15.3% on top of income tax). Set aside 25-35% of every payment for taxes. Open a separate savings account specifically for tax reserves.

### Quarterly Estimated Taxes

Most countries require self-employed individuals to pay estimated taxes quarterly rather than annually. Missing quarterly payments results in penalties and interest. Mark the due dates on your calendar and pay on time even if it means estimating conservatively.

### Deductible Expenses

Track every business expense:
- Home office (dedicated space only): proportional share of rent, utilities, internet
- Equipment: computer, software, subscriptions
- Professional development: courses, books, conferences
- Travel to client sites or conferences
- Health insurance premiums (often fully deductible for the self-employed)

Use accounting software (Wave, QuickBooks Self-Employed, or a simple spreadsheet) to categorize expenses throughout the year. Scrambling at tax time to reconstruct a year of expenses is stressful and inaccurate.

### Retirement Savings

Without an employer 401(k) match, you must fund retirement yourself. Options include:
- **SEP-IRA**: Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income. Simple to set up.
- **Solo 401(k)**: Higher contribution limits (employee + employer contributions). Worth the extra paperwork if your income is above $100,000.
- **Roth IRA**: Contribute post-tax dollars for tax-free growth and withdrawals. Income limits apply.

## Managing Your Time

### The Billable Hour Trap

Only about 60-70% of your working hours will be billable. The rest goes to invoicing, marketing, communication, learning, and administrative tasks. Account for this when planning your capacity — if you want 30 billable hours per week, expect to work 40-45 total hours.

### Boundaries

Working from home blurs the line between work and personal life. Set clear working hours and communicate them to clients. Having a dedicated workspace — even a specific desk in a corner — helps your brain switch between "work mode" and "off mode."

Respond to client messages during business hours only. Training clients to expect immediate responses at 10 PM sets an unsustainable precedent.

## Avoiding Burnout

Freelancing without boundaries leads to burnout faster than traditional employment because there's no external structure forcing you to stop. Take weekends off. Take vacation (you won't get paid, but you need the rest). Say no to projects that don't excite you or that come from difficult clients — the freedom to choose your work is the whole point of freelancing.
