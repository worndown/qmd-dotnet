# Vegetable Gardening Guide

## Planning Your Garden

Before planting anything, understand your growing conditions. Determine your USDA hardiness zone and your region's last frost date — this dictates when you can safely transplant warm-season crops outdoors. Most vegetables need at least 6-8 hours of direct sunlight per day. Observe your yard across the seasons to identify the sunniest spots.

### Raised Beds vs. In-Ground

Raised beds (20-30cm tall frames filled with garden soil) warm up faster in spring, drain better, and are easier to amend with compost. They're ideal for heavy clay or compacted soil. In-ground beds work well if you have naturally loose, fertile soil. Container gardening on a patio or balcony is viable for tomatoes, peppers, herbs, and lettuce — use at least 20-liter pots with drainage holes.

## Soil

Healthy soil is the foundation of a productive garden. Good garden soil is loamy — a balanced mix of sand, silt, and clay — and rich in organic matter. Get a soil test from your local extension service to check pH and nutrient levels.

### Amending Soil

Add 5-8 cm of compost to the top of your beds each spring and work it into the top 15 cm. Compost improves drainage in clay soils, improves water retention in sandy soils, and feeds the soil microbiome. Avoid adding raw manure directly to beds with edible crops — it can contain pathogens. Compost it first for at least 6 months.

### Mulching

Mulch the soil surface with 5-8 cm of straw, wood chips, or shredded leaves. Mulch suppresses weeds, retains moisture (reducing watering by 25-50%), and moderates soil temperature. Keep mulch a few centimeters away from plant stems to prevent rot.

## Watering

Most vegetables need 2.5 cm (1 inch) of water per week, including rainfall. Water deeply and less frequently rather than lightly every day — this encourages roots to grow deep, making plants more drought-resistant.

### Drip Irrigation

Drip irrigation delivers water directly to the root zone through emitters or soaker hoses. It reduces water waste (no evaporation from overhead sprinklers), keeps foliage dry (reducing fungal disease), and can be automated with a timer. Lay drip lines under the mulch for maximum efficiency.

### Signs of Water Stress

- **Wilting in the afternoon that recovers by morning**: Normal on hot days; not necessarily a sign to water more.
- **Wilting in the morning**: Underwatering or root damage.
- **Yellowing lower leaves**: Can indicate overwatering (similar to houseplants), especially in poorly drained soil.
- **Blossom end rot on tomatoes**: Not a disease — caused by inconsistent watering disrupting calcium uptake. Maintain even moisture.

## Planting Calendar

| Category | Examples | Start Indoors | Transplant/Direct Sow |
|----------|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| Cool-season | Lettuce, spinach, peas, radish, kale | 4-6 weeks before last frost | 2-4 weeks before last frost |
| Warm-season | Tomatoes, peppers, squash, beans, corn | 6-8 weeks before last frost | After last frost, soil > 15°C |
| Heat-loving | Melons, eggplant, okra, sweet potatoes | 8-10 weeks before last frost | 2 weeks after last frost, soil > 20°C |

Start seeds indoors under grow lights or on a bright windowsill. Harden off seedlings (gradually expose to outdoor conditions over 7-10 days) before transplanting to avoid transplant shock.

## Companion Planting

Some plants benefit from proximity to others:
- **Tomatoes + basil**: Basil may repel aphids and whiteflies; anecdotally improves tomato flavor
- **Beans + corn + squash** (Three Sisters): Beans fix nitrogen, corn provides a trellis for beans, squash leaves shade the soil and suppress weeds
- **Carrots + onions**: Onions repel carrot fly; carrots repel onion fly
- **Marigolds**: Plant around the garden perimeter — they deter nematodes and attract beneficial pollinators

Avoid planting nightshades (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant) in the same spot year after year — rotate crops on a 3-4 year cycle to prevent soilborne disease buildup.

## Common Pests

- **Aphids**: Small green or black insects clustered on new growth. Spray with a strong jet of water, or apply diluted neem oil or insecticidal soap.
- **Tomato hornworm**: Large green caterpillars that strip leaves overnight. Handpick them. If you see white cocoons on a hornworm, leave it — those are parasitic wasp eggs that will control future populations.
- **Slugs and snails**: Active at night, leaving slime trails and ragged holes in leaves. Use beer traps, copper tape around raised beds, or iron phosphate bait (safe for pets and wildlife).
- **Squash vine borer**: A moth larva that tunnels into squash stems, causing sudden wilt. Wrap the base of stems with aluminum foil as a barrier. Slit the stem and remove the larva if you catch it early.

## Harvesting

Pick vegetables at their peak: tomatoes fully colored but still firm, zucchini at 15-20 cm (larger ones are seedy and bland), lettuce leaves before the plant bolts (sends up a flower stalk, which turns leaves bitter). Harvesting frequently encourages continued production in beans, peppers, and squash.
