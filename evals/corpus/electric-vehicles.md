# Electric Vehicles: A Practical Guide

## How EVs Work

An electric vehicle replaces the internal combustion engine with one or more electric motors powered by a high-voltage lithium-ion battery pack. The motor converts electrical energy into torque with near-instant response — there is no transmission in most EVs (single-speed reduction gear), which eliminates gear shifts and provides seamless acceleration.

Regenerative braking recovers kinetic energy during deceleration: the motor runs in reverse as a generator, converting motion back into electricity and storing it in the battery. Regenerative braking can recover 10-25% of energy in typical driving, significantly extending range in stop-and-go city traffic.

## Battery Technology

### Lithium-Ion Chemistry

Most current EVs use one of three lithium-ion cathode chemistries:

- **NMC (Nickel Manganese Cobalt)**: High energy density, good power output. Used in most premium EVs (Tesla Model S/X, BMW, Mercedes). More expensive due to cobalt and nickel content.
- **LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate)**: Lower energy density but longer cycle life (3,000-5,000 cycles vs. 1,000-2,000 for NMC), lower cost, and no cobalt or nickel. Used in Tesla Model 3 Standard Range, BYD, and many Chinese EVs. Better thermal stability (reduced fire risk).
- **NCA (Nickel Cobalt Aluminum)**: Highest energy density. Used in Tesla's long-range packs. More sensitive to temperature and has shorter cycle life than LFP.

### Battery Degradation

Battery capacity gradually decreases over time and use. Most modern EV batteries retain 80-90% of original capacity after 200,000 km or 8-10 years. Factors that accelerate degradation:
- Frequent DC fast charging (high current generates heat)
- Keeping the battery at 100% state of charge for extended periods
- Extreme heat (sustained temperatures above 35°C)
- Deep discharges below 10%

### Optimal Charging Habits

For daily driving, charge to 80% and avoid going below 20%. Charge to 100% only when you need the full range for a long trip, and depart soon after reaching 100%. LFP batteries are an exception — they benefit from occasional full charges (100%) to allow the battery management system to recalibrate cell balancing.

## Charging Infrastructure

### Charging Levels

| Level | Power | Range Added Per Hour | Use Case |
|-------|-------|---------------------|----------|
| Level 1 (120V household outlet) | 1.4 kW | 5-8 km/hr | Emergency only |
| Level 2 (240V dedicated circuit) | 7-22 kW | 30-80 km/hr | Home/workplace daily charging |
| DC Fast Charging (Level 3) | 50-350 kW | 200-1,200 km/hr | Road trips, en-route charging |

### Home Charging

A Level 2 home charger (EVSE) on a dedicated 240V/40A circuit is the most convenient and cost-effective way to charge. Install it in your garage or driveway. Overnight charging (8 hours) replenishes 250-500 km of range depending on the EVSE's power output. Electricity costs are typically 3-5x cheaper per kilometer than gasoline.

### DC Fast Charging

DC fast chargers (CCS, CHAdeMO, Tesla Supercharger/NACS) bypass the car's onboard charger and deliver DC power directly to the battery. Charging speed depends on battery state of charge (fastest from 10-50%, slows significantly above 80%), ambient temperature, and the vehicle's maximum charge rate. A typical fast-charging stop adds 200 km in 20-30 minutes.

## Range Factors

The EPA-rated range is measured under controlled conditions. Real-world range varies based on:
- **Speed**: Aerodynamic drag increases with the square of speed. Driving at 130 km/h can reduce range by 25-30% compared to 100 km/h.
- **Temperature**: Cold weather (below 0°C) reduces range by 20-40% due to battery chemistry, cabin heating, and increased tire rolling resistance. Heat pumps (standard in newer EVs) mitigate heating losses.
- **Terrain**: Climbing a mountain pass drains the battery; descending recovers energy through regenerative braking. Net loss for mountainous routes is typically 10-15%.
- **HVAC**: Air conditioning and heating consume 1-3 kW. In extreme cold, heating can be the biggest single range drain.
- **Tire pressure**: Under-inflated tires increase rolling resistance. Check monthly and maintain the manufacturer's recommended pressure.

## Maintenance

EVs have dramatically lower maintenance needs than gasoline vehicles:
- **No oil changes**: No engine, no oil.
- **Brake pads last 2-3x longer**: Regenerative braking does most of the deceleration, reducing wear on friction brakes. Check pads for corrosion if they are rarely used.
- **Tire rotation every 10,000-12,000 km**: EVs are heavier than comparable ICE vehicles (battery weight), and instant torque accelerates tire wear. Rotate tires regularly and budget for slightly more frequent tire replacement.
- **Cabin air filter**: Replace every 30,000-50,000 km.
- **Coolant**: Battery thermal management coolant may need replacement every 5-8 years depending on manufacturer.
- **12V battery**: EVs still have a 12V auxiliary battery for electronics. It can fail like any car battery (3-5 year lifespan). Some EVs use lithium 12V batteries with longer lifespans.

## Total Cost of Ownership

While the purchase price of EVs is often higher than comparable ICE vehicles, the total cost of ownership over 5-8 years is often lower due to:
- Lower fuel costs (electricity vs. gasoline)
- Lower maintenance costs (fewer wear parts)
- Government incentives (tax credits, rebates, reduced registration fees)
- Higher resale value in some markets (strong demand for used EVs)

The breakeven point depends on your annual mileage, local electricity rates, and available incentives. For drivers covering 15,000+ km/year, the savings typically offset the higher purchase price within 3-5 years.
