# Rock Climbing Basics

## Climbing Disciplines

Rock climbing encompasses several distinct disciplines, each with its own gear, techniques, and culture.

### Bouldering

Bouldering involves climbing short routes (problems) on boulders or low walls, typically under 5 meters, without ropes. Crash pads placed below absorb falls. Problems are graded on the V-scale (V0-V17) in North America or the Font scale (4a-9a) in Europe. Bouldering develops power, technique, and problem-solving — each problem is a puzzle of body positions and hand sequences.

### Sport Climbing

Sport climbing uses pre-placed bolts drilled into the rock for protection. The lead climber clips quickdraws (two carabiners connected by a sewn sling) to the bolts while ascending. Falls are caught by the rope and belayer. Routes are graded on the Yosemite Decimal System (5.0-5.15d) in North America or the French scale (3-9c) internationally. Sport climbing emphasizes endurance and technical movement over long sequences.

### Trad Climbing

Traditional (trad) climbing requires the climber to place their own protection — cams, nuts, hexes, and other removable gear — into cracks and features in the rock. The second climber removes the gear while following. Trad climbing demands strong crack technique (jamming hands, fists, or feet into cracks), gear placement judgment, and mental fortitude. A poorly placed piece of protection may not hold a fall.

## Essential Knots

The figure-eight follow-through is the standard knot for tying into a climbing harness. Thread the rope through both tie-in points of the harness, then retrace the figure-eight knot. The finished knot should be neat with parallel strands, a tail of at least 15cm, and backed up with a stopper knot or fisherman's knot. Check your knot and your partner's knot before every climb — this is a non-negotiable safety ritual.

The clove hitch is used to anchor to a carabiner at a belay station. It's adjustable — you can lengthen or shorten the rope without untying. The munter hitch provides a friction-based belay method as a backup when a belay device is dropped.

## Belaying

Belaying is the act of managing the rope to protect a climbing partner. The belayer uses a belay device (ATC, GriGri, or similar) attached to their harness to control friction on the rope. The fundamental rule is to never let go of the brake strand — the portion of rope on the braking side of the device.

### Lead Belaying

When belaying a lead climber, pay out slack as they climb and clip bolts, but keep only minimal slack in the system. Too much slack means a longer fall; too little pulls the climber off balance or prevents them from clipping. If the climber falls, lock off the brake hand — an assisted-braking device like a GriGri provides a mechanical backup, but you must still hold the brake strand.

The belayer should stand slightly off to the side of the first bolt and be attentive to rope position. Giving a soft catch — taking a small step forward or hop during the catch — reduces the force on the climber and prevents them from slamming into the wall, especially on steep overhangs.

## Footwork and Technique

Good footwork is the single most impactful skill for climbing improvement. Beginners rely on arm strength; experienced climbers stand on their feet and use arms primarily for balance and positioning.

### Edging and Smearing

Edging uses the inside or outside edge of the climbing shoe on small ledges and features. Stand on the ball of the foot, directly over the big toe, for maximum precision. Smearing presses the flat of the shoe rubber against a featureless surface, relying on friction rather than a defined hold. Trust the rubber — modern climbing shoes use high-friction compounds (Vibram XS Grip2, Stealth C4) designed to stick to bare rock.

### Body Positioning

Keep your hips close to the wall to shift weight onto your feet. Flagging — extending a leg out to the side as a counterbalance — prevents barn-dooring (swinging away from the wall on overhangs). Drop knees and hip turns unlock reach on steep terrain by rotating the torso closer to the wall.

## Training for Climbing

### Finger Strength

Finger strength is the primary physical limiter in climbing. Hangboard training involves hanging from edges of decreasing size (20mm, 15mm, 10mm) for 7-10 second sets. Tendons adapt slower than muscles — spend at least 6 months of consistent climbing before starting hangboard training. Increase load gradually (2-5% per week) and never train through joint pain. Pulley injuries (particularly the A2 pulley in the ring finger) are the most common climbing-specific injury and can take 3-6 months to heal.

### Antagonist Training

Climbing creates muscle imbalances — overdeveloped pulling muscles (lats, biceps, forearm flexors) and underdeveloped pushing muscles (chest, triceps, forearm extensors). Balance this with push-ups, overhead press, wrist extensions, and reverse wrist curls. Neglecting antagonist training leads to shoulder impingement and elbow tendinitis (climber's elbow).

## Climbing Safety

Always perform a partner check before climbing: harness buckled and doubled back, knot tied correctly and through both tie-in points, belay device loaded correctly, carabiner locked. Helmets are mandatory for outdoor climbing — rockfall from above and pendulum falls near ledges cause head injuries.

Falls in climbing are routine, not emergencies. Practice falling on top rope and in the gym to become comfortable with the sensation. Communication between climber and belayer uses standard calls: "On belay?" / "Belay on." / "Climbing." / "Climb on." / "Take!" (hold my weight) / "Slack!" (feed rope) / "Lower!" (lower me down).
