# First Aid Essentials

## When to Call Emergency Services

Call emergency services (911, 112, or your local number) immediately for:
- Chest pain, difficulty breathing, or signs of stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty)
- Severe bleeding that won't stop with direct pressure
- Loss of consciousness
- Suspected spinal injury (do not move the person)
- Allergic reaction with throat swelling or difficulty breathing (anaphylaxis)
- Seizures lasting longer than 5 minutes or in someone with no history of seizures

When in doubt, call. It is always better to call and not need emergency services than to not call and need them.

## Wound Care

### Minor Cuts and Scrapes

1. Wash your hands before touching the wound
2. Stop the bleeding with gentle pressure using a clean cloth or gauze. Most minor wounds stop bleeding within 5-10 minutes.
3. Clean the wound under running water for 5 minutes. Remove visible debris with tweezers cleaned with alcohol. Do not use hydrogen peroxide or iodine directly in the wound — they damage healing tissue.
4. Apply a thin layer of antibiotic ointment (bacitracin or triple antibiotic) and cover with an adhesive bandage or sterile gauze
5. Change the dressing daily and whenever it gets wet or dirty

### When Stitches Are Needed

Seek medical attention if: the wound is deeper than 6mm, the edges don't come together naturally, the wound is on the face or over a joint, you can see fat or muscle tissue, or bleeding doesn't stop after 15 minutes of firm pressure. Stitches should ideally be placed within 6-8 hours of injury.

## Burns

### Burn Classification

- **First degree**: Red, painful skin without blisters (like a sunburn). Affects only the outer skin layer (epidermis).
- **Second degree**: Blisters, intense pain, red and wet-looking skin. Affects the epidermis and part of the dermis.
- **Third degree**: White, brown, or charred skin that may be numb (nerve endings destroyed). Requires emergency medical treatment.

### Treatment

For first and second degree burns: run cool (not ice-cold) water over the burn for 10-20 minutes. This is the single most effective first aid intervention for burns. Do not apply ice, butter, toothpaste, or any home remedy — these trap heat and increase tissue damage. Cover loosely with a sterile non-stick dressing. Over-the-counter ibuprofen manages pain and inflammation. Do not pop blisters — they protect the healing skin beneath.

## CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

CPR keeps oxygenated blood flowing to the brain until professional help arrives. Brain damage begins within 4-6 minutes of cardiac arrest. Bystander CPR doubles or triples survival rates.

### Hands-Only CPR

For adults who suddenly collapse:
1. Check for responsiveness — tap the shoulders and shout. If no response, call emergency services (or have someone else call).
2. Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest (between the nipples), place the other hand on top, and interlock fingers.
3. Push hard and fast — at least 5 cm (2 inches) deep, at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute (the tempo of "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees).
4. Allow full chest recoil between compressions — don't lean on the chest.
5. Continue until emergency services arrive or an AED is available.

### AED (Automated External Defibrillator)

AEDs are found in many public places (airports, gyms, offices). They analyze the heart rhythm and deliver a shock if needed. Turn it on — it provides voice instructions. Attach the pads to bare, dry skin as shown on the pad diagrams. The AED will not shock a normal rhythm; you cannot harm the person by using it.

## Choking

### Adult / Child (Over 1 Year)

If the person can cough forcefully, encourage them to keep coughing. If they cannot cough, speak, or breathe:
1. Stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist
2. Make a fist with one hand and place it just above the navel, below the ribcage
3. Grasp the fist with your other hand and thrust inward and upward sharply
4. Repeat until the object is expelled or the person loses consciousness (then begin CPR)

### Infant (Under 1 Year)

Hold the infant face-down on your forearm, supporting the head. Deliver 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand. If the object doesn't come out, turn the infant face-up and give 5 chest thrusts (two fingers on the center of the chest, same position as infant CPR). Alternate between 5 back blows and 5 chest thrusts until the object clears.

## Sprains and Fractures

### RICE Protocol

For sprains (ligament injuries) and strains (muscle/tendon injuries):
- **Rest**: Stop the activity immediately. Continued use worsens the injury.
- **Ice**: Apply ice wrapped in a cloth for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours for the first 48 hours. Never apply ice directly to skin.
- **Compression**: Wrap with an elastic bandage to reduce swelling. Not tight enough to cut off circulation — check for numbness, tingling, or blue discoloration.
- **Elevation**: Raise the injured limb above heart level when possible.

### Suspected Fracture

If a limb is visibly deformed, there is severe pain with any movement, or you heard a snap: immobilize the area above and below the suspected fracture (splint with a rigid object — a rolled magazine, a board, or a SAM splint). Do not attempt to realign the bone. Apply ice and seek medical attention.

## First Aid Kit Contents

A basic home and travel first aid kit should include:
- Adhesive bandages (various sizes)
- Sterile gauze pads and adhesive tape
- Elastic bandage (ACE wrap)
- Antibiotic ointment
- Hydrocortisone cream (for insect bites and rashes)
- Ibuprofen and acetaminophen
- Tweezers and small scissors
- Disposable gloves
- Instant cold pack
- CPR pocket mask
- Emergency blanket
- List of emergency contacts and any medical conditions/allergies
