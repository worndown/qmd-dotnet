# Sourdough Bread Baking

## Understanding the Starter

A sourdough starter is a living culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria maintained in a flour-and-water mixture. Unlike commercial yeast, which is a single strain of Saccharomyces cerevisiae, a sourdough starter contains a diverse microbial ecosystem. The bacteria produce lactic and acetic acids, giving sourdough its characteristic tang.

A healthy starter roughly doubles in volume within 4-8 hours of feeding. It should smell pleasantly sour — like yogurt or mild vinegar — not like nail polish remover (which indicates excess acetic acid from under-feeding).

### Feeding Schedule

Feed your starter equal parts flour and water by weight (1:1:1 ratio — starter:flour:water). At room temperature (20-24°C), feed every 12 hours. In the refrigerator, weekly feeding is sufficient for maintenance. Take the starter out the night before baking and give it two room-temperature feeds to ensure peak activity.

### The Float Test

To check if your starter is ready, drop a small spoonful into water. If it floats, the culture has produced enough gas to leaven bread. If it sinks, give it another hour or do another feed.

## The Autolyse

Autolyse is a rest period after mixing flour and water (without starter or salt) that allows the flour to fully hydrate and gluten to begin developing passively. Typically 30-60 minutes. This reduces the total mixing time needed and produces a more extensible dough.

## Bulk Fermentation

After combining the autolysed dough with starter and salt, bulk fermentation begins. This is the primary rise, lasting 4-6 hours at room temperature. During this phase, perform a series of stretch-and-fold sets every 30-45 minutes for the first 2 hours. Each set involves stretching one side of the dough up and folding it over itself, rotating the bowl 90 degrees and repeating for all four sides.

The dough is ready when it has increased in volume by roughly 50-75%, feels airy, and shows bubbles on the surface and along the sides of the container.

## Shaping

Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. For a round boule, use a bench scraper to drag the dough toward you, creating surface tension as the bottom grips the counter. For a batard (oval), first shape into a rough rectangle, fold the top third down and the bottom third up, then roll tightly and seal the seam.

Surface tension is critical — it's what gives the loaf structure and a tall rise in the oven. If the dough tears during shaping, it needs more gluten development.

## Cold Retard

Place the shaped dough seam-side up in a floured banneton (proofing basket) and refrigerate for 12-18 hours. This slow, cold fermentation develops complex flavors — the longer the retard, the more sour the bread. It also makes the dough easier to score.

## Baking

Preheat your oven to 250°C (480°F) with a Dutch oven inside for at least 45 minutes. Score the cold dough with a razor blade (lame) — a single ear-shaped cut along the side is classic. Transfer to the Dutch oven, cover with the lid, and bake for 20 minutes. Remove the lid and continue baking at 230°C (445°F) for another 20-25 minutes until the crust is deep mahogany.

The steam trapped under the lid allows the crust to stay flexible during oven spring, enabling maximum rise before the crust sets.

## Crumb Troubleshooting

- **Dense, gummy crumb**: Under-fermented. Extend bulk fermentation or use a warmer spot.
- **Large irregular holes**: Over-fermented or insufficient shaping tension. Shorten bulk or work on shaping technique.
- **Flat loaf, no ear**: Weak starter, over-proofing, or insufficient surface tension during shaping.
- **Thick, pale crust**: Oven not hot enough or not enough steam. Ensure thorough preheating.

## Hydration Percentages

Hydration is the ratio of water to flour by weight. A 75% hydration dough uses 750g water per 1000g flour. Higher hydration produces a more open crumb but is harder to handle. Beginners should start at 70% and work up.

| Hydration | Character |
|-----------|-----------|
| 65-70% | Easy to handle, tighter crumb |
| 70-75% | Good balance of openness and workability |
| 75-80% | Open crumb, sticky dough, requires experience |
| 80%+ | Very open crumb, ciabatta territory |
