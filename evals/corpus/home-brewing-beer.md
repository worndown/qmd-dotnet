# Home Brewing Beer

## The Brewing Process Overview

Beer is made from four ingredients: water, malted grain, hops, and yeast. The process involves extracting sugars from grain (mashing), boiling with hops for bitterness and aroma, cooling, adding yeast, and fermenting for 1-3 weeks. There are two main approaches: extract brewing (using pre-made malt extract) and all-grain brewing (mashing raw malted barley yourself).

## Water Chemistry

Water makes up over 90% of beer, and its mineral content affects flavor, mash pH, and yeast health. The key minerals are calcium (50-150 ppm for enzyme activity), sulfate (enhances hop bitterness, crisp finish), and chloride (enhances malt sweetness, body). The sulfate-to-chloride ratio shapes the beer's character: a 2:1 ratio suits hoppy IPAs, while 1:2 suits malty stouts.

Mash pH should be 5.2-5.6 for efficient starch conversion. If your water is alkaline, add food-grade lactic acid or acidulated malt. Use brewing water calculators (Bru'n Water, Brewfather) to adjust your source water to match target profiles — Burton-on-Trent water for pale ales, Pilsen water for lagers.

## Mashing (All-Grain)

Mashing is soaking crushed malted barley in hot water (the mash) to activate enzymes that convert starch to fermentable sugar. The standard single-infusion mash holds at 64-68°C (148-155°F) for 60 minutes. Temperature controls the fermentability of the wort:

- **Lower range (62-65°C)**: Beta-amylase dominates, producing more fermentable sugars. Results in a drier, thinner beer.
- **Higher range (66-70°C)**: Alpha-amylase dominates, producing more unfermentable dextrins. Results in a sweeter, fuller-bodied beer.

After mashing, sparge (rinse) the grain bed with 76°C (168°F) water to extract remaining sugars. Fly sparging slowly rinses from the top while draining from the bottom; batch sparging adds water in batches and drains completely between additions. Both methods target a pre-boil gravity of your recipe's specification.

## The Boil

Bring the wort to a rolling boil for 60-90 minutes. The boil sterilizes the wort, isomerizes alpha acids from hops into bitter iso-alpha acids, drives off volatile off-flavors (notably DMS, dimethyl sulfide, which tastes like cooked corn), and concentrates the wort through evaporation.

### Hop Additions

Hops added at different boil times serve different purposes:

- **Bittering hops (60 minutes)**: Long boil fully isomerizes alpha acids. Contributes bitterness measured in IBUs (International Bitterness Units).
- **Flavor hops (15-30 minutes)**: Partial isomerization plus retained flavor compounds.
- **Aroma hops (0-5 minutes / flameout)**: Minimal bitterness, maximum aromatic oils.
- **Dry hopping (post-fermentation)**: Hops steeped in finished beer for 3-7 days. Adds intense aroma without bitterness. Essential for modern IPAs and NEIPAs.

## Fermentation

After boiling, cool the wort rapidly to pitching temperature using an immersion chiller, counterflow chiller, or ice bath. Rapid cooling reduces the risk of contamination and causes proteins and tannins to precipitate (cold break), improving clarity.

### Yeast Selection

Yeast is the single biggest factor in beer flavor beyond the recipe itself.

- **Ale yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae)**: Ferments at 15-22°C, produces fruity esters and phenolic flavors. Fermentation completes in 5-10 days.
- **Lager yeast (Saccharomyces pastorianus)**: Ferments at 8-14°C, produces cleaner, crisper flavors with fewer esters. Requires 2-4 weeks plus a cold conditioning (lagering) period at 0-4°C for 2-6 weeks.
- **Wild yeast and bacteria**: Brettanomyces, Lactobacillus, and Pediococcus produce sour, funky beers (lambics, Berliner Weisse, Flanders red). These fermentations can take months to years.

Pitch rate matters: under-pitching causes stressed yeast to produce excessive esters and fusel alcohols. A standard pitch rate is 0.75 million cells per milliliter per degree Plato for ales, and 1.5 million for lagers. Use a yeast calculator and make a starter if needed — boil 100g of dried malt extract in 1 liter of water, cool, pitch a yeast packet, and let it grow on a stir plate for 24-48 hours.

### Fermentation Control

Temperature stability is more important than hitting an exact number. A 5°C swing can produce unwanted off-flavors. Use a fermentation chamber (a chest freezer or mini-fridge with an Inkbird temperature controller) to hold the fermenter at the target temperature. Fermentation itself generates heat — the beer inside may be 2-4°C warmer than the ambient temperature.

## Bottling and Carbonation

The simplest packaging method is bottle conditioning: dissolve priming sugar (typically 4-5 grams of corn sugar per liter) in the finished beer, fill sanitized bottles, cap them, and store at room temperature for 2-3 weeks. Residual yeast ferments the priming sugar, producing CO2 that dissolves into the beer under pressure. Use a priming sugar calculator — too much sugar risks bottle bombs (dangerous glass shrapnel).

Kegging with a CO2 tank allows force-carbonation: set the regulator to 12-14 PSI at fridge temperature (2-4°C) and wait 5-7 days, or use the burst carbonation method (30 PSI for 24 hours, then reduce to serving pressure).

## Sanitation

Every surface that contacts the wort after the boil must be sanitized. Star San (phosphoric acid and surfactant) is the standard no-rinse sanitizer — mix 1.5ml per liter of water. Contact time is 30 seconds. It foams aggressively but the foam is safe and actually beneficial in fermenters. Infections from wild bacteria or yeast produce off-flavors (sourness, vinegar, band-aid phenolics) that ruin a batch. When in doubt, sanitize it.
