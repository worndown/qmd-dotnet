# Hiking and Backpacking

## Gear Essentials

### The Big Three

The three heaviest items in a backpacking setup are the shelter, sleeping system, and backpack. For multi-day trips, aim for a combined weight of 3-5 kg for the big three. Ultralight setups push below 3 kg by using single-wall shelters (Zpacks Duplex, Durston X-Mid), quilt-style sleeping bags (Enlightened Equipment Enigma), and frameless packs (Gossamer Gear Mariposa).

### Shelter

- **Freestanding tents**: Two-pole designs that stand without stakes. Heavier but easy to pitch on any surface. Good for beginners and rocky terrain.
- **Trekking pole shelters**: Use trekking poles as tent poles, saving 300-500g. Require staking out for structure.
- **Hammock systems**: A hammock with a rainfly (tarp) and underquilt replaces both tent and sleeping pad. Comfortable but limited to areas with trees spaced 3-5 meters apart.
- **Bivy sacks**: Minimalist waterproof shells that fit over a sleeping bag. Maximum weight savings but claustrophobic and prone to condensation.

### Sleep System

Match your sleeping bag or quilt to expected nighttime temperatures. Temperature ratings are survival ratings, not comfort ratings — buy a bag rated 5-10°C below the coldest temperature you expect. Down insulation (650-900 fill power) offers the best warmth-to-weight ratio but loses insulation when wet. Synthetic insulation (Climashield Apex, Primaloft) retains warmth when damp and costs less but is bulkier and heavier.

A sleeping pad provides both comfort and critical insulation from the cold ground. R-value measures thermal resistance: R-2 for summer, R-3.5 for three-season, R-5+ for winter. Inflatable pads (Therm-a-Rest NeoAir XLite) are lightweight and packable; closed-cell foam pads (Z Lite Sol) are indestructible and double as sit pads but are bulkier.

## Navigation

### Map and Compass

A topographic map and compass are the most reliable navigation tools — they don't require batteries, a signal, or satellites. Learn to take a bearing: align the compass edge between your current position and destination on the map, rotate the bezel until the orienting lines align with the map's north-south grid lines, then hold the compass flat and rotate your body until the magnetic needle aligns with the orienting arrow. Walk the bearing.

Declination — the difference between magnetic north and true north — varies by location and must be accounted for. In the western US, magnetic north is east of true north by 10-15°. Set the declination adjustment on your compass or manually add/subtract when converting between map bearings and field bearings.

### GPS and Apps

Smartphone GPS apps (Gaia GPS, AllTrails, CalTopo) are excellent secondary navigation tools. Download offline maps and track your route. Carry a battery bank (10,000+ mAh) and keep the phone in airplane mode to extend battery life. A dedicated GPS device (Garmin inReach Mini) provides satellite messaging and SOS capability in areas without cell service.

## Water Purification

Never drink untreated water from streams, lakes, or springs, regardless of how clear it looks. Pathogens include Giardia, Cryptosporidium, bacteria (E. coli, Salmonella), and viruses (less common in North American backcountry).

### Purification Methods

- **Gravity filter** (Sawyer Squeeze, Platypus GravityWorks): Filters bacteria and protozoa. Fast and requires no chemicals. Replace or backflush regularly.
- **Chemical treatment** (Aquamira chlorine dioxide, Katadyn Micropur): Kills all pathogens including viruses. Wait 15-30 minutes after treatment. Adds a slight taste.
- **UV purification** (SteriPen): Disrupts pathogen DNA in 60-90 seconds. Requires batteries and clear water (turbid water blocks UV).
- **Boiling**: Bring water to a rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes above 2,000 meters elevation). Guaranteed to kill all pathogens but requires fuel and time.

## Leave No Trace Principles

The seven Leave No Trace principles are the ethical framework for backcountry travel:

1. **Plan ahead and prepare**: Check regulations, weather, and trail conditions before departure.
2. **Travel on durable surfaces**: Stay on established trails. Walk through mud puddles rather than around them — going around widens the trail.
3. **Dispose of waste properly**: Pack out all trash, food scraps, and toilet paper. Dig a cathole 15-20 cm deep and at least 60 meters from water, trails, and campsites for human waste.
4. **Leave what you find**: Don't take rocks, flowers, or artifacts.
5. **Minimize campfire impacts**: Use a stove instead of a fire. If fires are permitted, use an existing fire ring and burn only small, dead wood.
6. **Respect wildlife**: Observe from a distance. Store food in a bear canister or hang it from a bear line at least 3 meters off the ground and 1.5 meters from the trunk.
7. **Be considerate of other visitors**: Yield to uphill hikers. Keep noise down, especially near campsites.

## Foot Care

Blisters are the most common backcountry injury and can end a trip. Prevention is key: wear well-broken-in boots or trail runners that fit with room for toe splay when descending. Moisture breeds blisters — use moisture-wicking merino wool or synthetic socks, never cotton. Apply Leukotape or moleskin to hot spots (reddened, irritated areas) immediately, before a blister forms.

If a blister develops, clean the area with an alcohol wipe, drain it with a sterilized needle at the base (don't remove the roof of skin — it's a natural bandage), apply antibiotic ointment, and cover with a hydrocolloid bandage (Compeed) or moleskin donut.

## Weather and Hypothermia

Hypothermia occurs when core body temperature drops below 35°C (95°F). It can develop at temperatures well above freezing — wet clothing and wind dramatically accelerate heat loss. The phrase "cotton kills" is backcountry gospel: wet cotton has zero insulating value and clings to skin, wicking away warmth. Wear synthetic or merino wool base layers, and carry a waterproof shell.

Early signs include shivering, clumsiness, and slurred speech. Treatment: get out of wind and rain, replace wet clothing, add insulation, provide warm (not hot) fluids, and share body heat if necessary.
