# Guitar for Beginners

## Choosing a Guitar

### Acoustic vs. Electric

Start with whichever type plays the music you love — motivation matters more than theoretical advantages. Acoustic guitars need no amplifier, are portable, and build finger strength faster due to higher string tension. Electric guitars have thinner necks and lower action (string height), making them physically easier to play. Classical (nylon-string) guitars are gentlest on fingers but have wider necks.

### What to Look For

- **Action**: The distance between the strings and the fretboard. High action makes pressing strings painful. Have a guitar shop set up the action if it's too high.
- **Intonation**: Play the 12th-fret harmonic and then the fretted 12th fret — they should be the same pitch. If not, the guitar has intonation problems.
- **Size**: Full-size (4/4) for adults. Children and smaller players should consider 3/4 size. A guitar that's too large causes bad posture and hand strain.

## Tuning

Standard tuning from lowest (thickest) to highest (thinnest) string is E-A-D-G-B-E. Use a clip-on tuner or a phone app — tuning by ear comes later. Always tune up to pitch rather than down. If the string is sharp, detune below the target note and tune back up. This seats the string in the nut slot and keeps tuning stable.

### Alternate Tunings

- **Drop D**: Lower the 6th string from E to D. Allows heavy power chords with one finger across the bottom three strings. Common in rock and metal.
- **Open G** (D-G-D-G-B-D): Strum all strings for a G major chord. Used extensively in blues and slide guitar.
- **DADGAD**: A popular Celtic/folk tuning that creates a rich, droning sound.

## Basic Chords

### Open Chords

Open chords use a combination of fretted notes and open strings. The first chords to learn:

- **E minor (Em)**: Fingers on 2nd fret of the A and D strings. Easiest chord on the guitar.
- **A minor (Am)**: First finger on 1st fret of B string, second finger on 2nd fret of D string, third finger on 2nd fret of G string.
- **C major**: Similar to Am but the ring finger moves to the 3rd fret of the A string. A common stumbling block — the fingertip must arch over the high E string without muting it.
- **G major**: Multiple fingerings exist. The 4-finger version (fingers on frets 2, 3, and 3) produces the fullest sound.
- **D major**: Three fingers clustered in a triangle shape on the top three strings.

With Em, Am, C, G, and D, you can play hundreds of popular songs.

### Barre Chords

Barre chords use one finger (usually the index) to press all six strings across a fret, with the remaining fingers forming a chord shape. The F major barre chord (first fret) is the first major hurdle for beginners. It requires significant grip strength and precise finger placement. Move the same shape up the neck to play any major chord — the root note on the low E string names the chord.

## Strumming

Hold the pick between thumb and index finger with about 5mm of the tip showing. Strum from the wrist, not the elbow. Start with simple downstrokes on every beat (1-2-3-4). Then add upstrokes on the "and" beats (1-and-2-and-3-and-4-and) for a basic eighth-note pattern.

### Common Strum Patterns

- **D-D-U-U-D-U** (down-down-up-up-down-up): The most versatile pop/folk pattern. Works over almost anything.
- **D-U-x-U-D-U** (with a muted strum on beat 3): Adds a percussive element. Mute by relaxing the fretting hand on the "x" beat.

## Fingerpicking

Instead of a pick, pluck individual strings with the thumb and fingers. The thumb handles the bass strings (E, A, D) and the index, middle, and ring fingers handle the G, B, and high E strings respectively.

A simple fingerpicking pattern: thumb plays the bass note on beat 1, then index-middle-ring pluck in sequence on beats 2-3-4. Travis picking alternates the bass note between the root and the fifth of the chord, creating a rolling, rhythmic feel characteristic of folk and country music.

## Reading Tabs

Guitar tablature (tabs) is a simplified notation system. Six lines represent the six strings (bottom line = low E). Numbers on the lines indicate which fret to press. Zero means play the string open. Tabs don't indicate rhythm precisely — listen to the recording alongside the tab.

## Practice Tips

- **15 minutes daily beats 2 hours on weekends**: Muscle memory builds through consistent repetition, not marathon sessions.
- **Use a metronome**: Start slow (60 BPM) and only increase speed when you can play cleanly at the current tempo. Speed is a byproduct of accuracy, not the other way around.
- **Isolate problem spots**: If you stumble on one chord change (e.g., G to C), spend 5 minutes drilling just that transition instead of playing the whole song poorly.
- **Record yourself**: Listen back critically. You'll catch timing errors and muted strings that you miss in the moment.
- **Play songs you love**: Technical exercises build skill, but playing actual songs builds motivation. Learn songs early and often.

## Common Beginner Problems

- **Buzzing strings**: Fingers not pressing hard enough or not close enough to the fret wire. Press just behind the fret, not on top of it.
- **Muted strings**: Adjacent fingers touching strings they shouldn't. Arch the fingers so only the very tips contact the strings.
- **Sore fingertips**: Normal for the first 2-3 weeks. Calluses form and the pain disappears. Don't practice through blisters — take a day off and let them heal.
- **Slow chord changes**: Completely normal. Lift all fingers simultaneously, move as a unit to the new shape, and place them all at once rather than one at a time.
