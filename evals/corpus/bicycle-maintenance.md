# Bicycle Maintenance Guide

## Pre-Ride Check (ABC Quick Check)

Before every ride, run through the ABC checklist:

- **A — Air**: Squeeze both tires. They should feel firm. Check the sidewall for the recommended pressure range (usually 80-130 PSI for road bikes, 30-50 PSI for mountain bikes). Use a floor pump with a gauge — hand-squeezing is unreliable. Under-inflated tires increase rolling resistance and risk pinch flats.
- **B — Brakes**: Squeeze each brake lever. The pads should contact the rim (or rotor) firmly before the lever reaches the handlebar. Spin each wheel and check that the brakes don't rub. Check brake pad wear — most rim brake pads have wear indicator grooves; replace them when the grooves disappear.
- **C — Chain**: The chain should be clean and lightly oiled. A dry, rusty chain wears out the drivetrain prematurely and shifts poorly. A dirty, gunky chain attracts grit that accelerates wear.
- **Quick releases / thru-axles**: Ensure wheels are secure. A loose wheel is a crash waiting to happen.

## Chain Maintenance

The chain is the most maintenance-intensive component. A clean, lubricated chain shifts smoothly, runs quietly, and extends the life of the more expensive cassette and chainrings.

### Cleaning

Wipe the chain with a rag after every ride to remove surface grit. Every 200-300 km (or sooner if you've ridden in rain or dirt), do a deep clean: apply degreaser to the chain while backpedaling, scrub with a chain cleaning tool or old toothbrush, rinse with water, and dry thoroughly before re-lubricating.

### Lubrication

Apply one drop of chain lube to each link while slowly backpedaling. Wait 5 minutes for the lube to penetrate, then wipe off the excess with a clean rag. Excess lube on the outside of the chain attracts dirt and does nothing useful — the lube needs to be inside the rollers and pins.

- **Dry lube**: Wax-based. Stays cleaner, attracts less grit. Best for dry conditions. Washes off easily in rain.
- **Wet lube**: Oil-based. Lasts through rain and mud. Attracts more dirt and requires more frequent cleaning.

### Chain Wear

A chain stretches (wears) over time as the pins and rollers grind against each other. A chain checker tool measures this stretch. Replace the chain when it reaches 0.5% elongation (0.75% for 11-speed and above). Riding on a worn chain destroys the cassette and chainrings — replacing a $20 chain is much cheaper than replacing a $60-120 cassette.

## Tire and Tube Care

### Fixing a Flat

Carry a spare tube, tire levers, and a mini pump or CO2 inflator on every ride.

1. Remove the wheel. Release the brake (rim brakes) or open the quick release/thru-axle.
2. Use tire levers to unseat the tire bead from the rim. Start opposite the valve.
3. Remove the old tube. Inflate it to find the puncture — listen or feel for air, or submerge in water.
4. Check the inside of the tire for the object that caused the flat (thorn, glass, wire). Run your finger carefully along the inside. Remove it before inserting the new tube, or you'll get another flat immediately.
5. Slightly inflate the new tube (just enough to give it shape) and insert it into the tire, starting with the valve through the rim hole.
6. Reseat the tire bead onto the rim using your hands — avoid tire levers at this stage, which can pinch the tube.
7. Inflate to the recommended pressure.

### Tire Pressure

Higher pressure reduces rolling resistance on smooth pavement but transmits more road vibration. Lower pressure improves grip and comfort on rough surfaces. Modern research suggests that slightly lower pressures (10-15% below maximum) often result in faster real-world speeds due to reduced energy lost to vibration.

## Brake Adjustment

### Rim Brakes

Brake pads should hit the braking surface of the rim squarely — not overlapping onto the tire (which will cut the tire) or dipping below the rim. Toe-in the pads slightly (front of the pad contacts the rim a hair before the rear) to eliminate squealing. Adjust cable tension with the barrel adjuster at the brake lever: turning it counter-clockwise takes up slack and brings the pads closer.

### Disc Brakes

Disc brakes require less frequent adjustment but are more sensitive to contamination. Never touch the rotor or pads with bare hands — skin oils reduce braking power. If brakes squeal, clean the rotor with isopropyl alcohol. If pads are contaminated (oil, spray lubricant), replace them — contaminated disc pads cannot be reliably restored.

To align disc calipers: loosen the caliper mounting bolts, squeeze the brake lever to center the caliper on the rotor, and tighten the bolts while holding the lever. Spin the wheel and listen for rubbing. Fine-tune with the caliper adjustment screws if your brake has them.

## Derailleur Adjustment

Poor shifting is usually caused by cable tension, not a bent derailleur. If the chain hesitates to shift to a larger cog (harder to pedal), the cable is too loose — turn the barrel adjuster counter-clockwise one quarter turn at a time. If it hesitates shifting to a smaller cog, the cable is too tight — turn clockwise.

The limit screws (marked H and L) prevent the derailleur from throwing the chain off the cassette. Set the H (high) limit with the chain on the smallest cog: the upper pulley should align directly below the smallest cog. Set the L (low) limit with the chain on the largest cog. Only touch limit screws if the chain is at risk of going off the ends of the cassette.

## Seasonal Service

Every 2,000-3,000 km or annually (whichever comes first), perform a full service:
- Replace brake pads and cables (rim brakes) or bleed hydraulic fluid (disc brakes)
- Replace chain (if worn past 0.5%)
- Check cassette and chainring teeth for shark-fin wear profiles
- Repack or replace wheel hub bearings
- Regrease headset bearings
- Check spoke tension (squeeze pairs of spokes — similar tension across each wheel)
- Replace handlebar tape or grips
- True wheels if they wobble laterally
