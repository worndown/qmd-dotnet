# Sleep and Recovery Science

## Why Sleep Matters

Sleep is the single most effective recovery tool available. During sleep, the body repairs muscle tissue, consolidates memories, regulates hormones, and clears metabolic waste from the brain via the glymphatic system. Chronic sleep deprivation (fewer than 6 hours per night) is linked to impaired cognitive function, weakened immune response, increased injury risk, weight gain, and higher all-cause mortality.

Athletes who sleep fewer than 7 hours per night are 1.7 times more likely to be injured than those sleeping 8+ hours.

## Sleep Architecture

A full night of sleep consists of 4-6 cycles, each lasting approximately 90 minutes. Each cycle progresses through four stages:

1. **N1 (light sleep)**: Transition from wakefulness. Lasts 1-5 minutes. Easily awakened.
2. **N2 (moderate sleep)**: Heart rate and body temperature drop. Sleep spindles (bursts of neural activity) consolidate motor learning. Makes up 50% of total sleep.
3. **N3 (deep sleep / slow-wave sleep)**: The most restorative stage. Growth hormone is released, muscle tissue is repaired, and the immune system is bolstered. Predominates in the first half of the night.
4. **REM (rapid eye movement)**: Brain activity resembles wakefulness. Dreaming occurs. Emotional regulation and creative problem-solving are consolidated. Predominates in the second half of the night.

Alcohol suppresses REM sleep. Even moderate drinking (2 drinks) in the evening reduces REM by up to 20%, impairing cognitive recovery regardless of total sleep duration.

## Circadian Rhythm

The circadian rhythm is an internal 24-hour clock regulated primarily by light exposure. The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus responds to light detected by specialized retinal ganglion cells (melanopsin receptors), which signal the pineal gland to suppress or release melatonin.

### Morning Light

Exposure to bright light (ideally sunlight, 10,000+ lux) within 30-60 minutes of waking anchors the circadian clock and promotes alertness. Indoor lighting is typically 100-500 lux — insufficient for a strong circadian signal. On overcast days, outdoor light still reaches 1,000-5,000 lux.

### Evening Light Hygiene

Blue-enriched light from screens suppresses melatonin production. Dim lights 1-2 hours before bed. Blue-light-blocking glasses provide some benefit, but the most effective strategy is simply reducing overall light intensity in the evening. Avoid overhead lights; use lamps at eye level or below.

## Sleep Hygiene Practices

- **Consistent schedule**: Go to bed and wake up at the same time every day, including weekends. Social jet lag (sleeping in on weekends) disrupts circadian alignment.
- **Cool bedroom**: 18-19°C (65-67°F) is optimal. Core body temperature must drop to initiate sleep. A warm bath 90 minutes before bed paradoxically helps — it brings blood to the surface and accelerates heat dissipation.
- **Dark room**: Use blackout curtains. Even dim light through closed eyelids can suppress melatonin. Cover LED indicator lights.
- **No caffeine after 2 PM**: Caffeine has a half-life of 5-6 hours. A 3 PM coffee means half the caffeine is still circulating at 9 PM.
- **Wind-down routine**: 30-60 minutes of non-stimulating activity before bed — reading, stretching, journaling. Avoid work emails or social media.

## Napping

A 20-minute nap (power nap) boosts alertness and performance without entering deep sleep, which causes grogginess (sleep inertia) on waking. The ideal nap window is early afternoon (1-3 PM), coinciding with the post-lunch circadian dip. Napping after 3 PM can interfere with nighttime sleep onset.

A 90-minute nap covers a full sleep cycle including deep and REM sleep, and is useful when recovering from acute sleep debt. However, regular long naps may indicate insufficient nighttime sleep.

## Sleep and Athletic Performance

Studies show that extending sleep to 10 hours per night for 5-7 weeks improves sprint times, shooting accuracy, reaction time, and mood in collegiate athletes. Sleep restriction studies show the opposite: a single night of 4 hours of sleep reduces maximal voluntary strength by 10-15% and aerobic endurance by 20-30%.

Sleep also mediates hormonal recovery:
- **Growth hormone**: 70% of daily GH secretion occurs during N3 deep sleep
- **Testosterone**: Sleep restriction reduces testosterone levels by 10-15% — comparable to 10-15 years of aging
- **Cortisol**: Poor sleep elevates cortisol, impairing muscle protein synthesis and promoting fat storage

## Common Sleep Disorders

- **Insomnia**: Difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking too early. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is the first-line treatment — more effective than medication long-term and without side effects.
- **Sleep apnea**: Repeated airway obstruction during sleep, causing oxygen desaturation and fragmented sleep. Symptoms include loud snoring, gasping, and excessive daytime sleepiness. Diagnosed via sleep study; treated with CPAP or oral appliances.
- **Restless leg syndrome**: Uncomfortable sensations in the legs with an irresistible urge to move, worse at night. Associated with iron deficiency. Check ferritin levels.

## Tracking Sleep

Consumer sleep trackers (Oura ring, Whoop, Apple Watch) provide reasonable estimates of total sleep time but are less accurate for sleep staging. They are useful for identifying trends — consistently low sleep scores correlate with subjective feelings of fatigue and reduced performance. However, obsessing over nightly sleep scores can itself cause anxiety that impairs sleep (orthosomnia).
