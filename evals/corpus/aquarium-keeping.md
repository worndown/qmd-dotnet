# Aquarium Keeping

## The Nitrogen Cycle

The nitrogen cycle is the most important concept in aquarium keeping. Fish excrete ammonia (NH3/NH4+) through their gills and waste. Ammonia is highly toxic — concentrations above 0.02 ppm stress fish, and above 1 ppm can be lethal within days. Beneficial bacteria (Nitrosomonas) colonize filter media and convert ammonia to nitrite (NO2-), which is also toxic. A second group of bacteria (Nitrospira) converts nitrite to nitrate (NO3-), which is relatively harmless below 40 ppm.

Cycling a new tank means establishing these bacterial colonies before adding fish. The fishless cycling method adds pure ammonia (dose to 2 ppm) and waits 4-6 weeks for the bacteria to establish. Monitor with a liquid test kit (API Master Test Kit) — when both ammonia and nitrite consistently read 0 ppm and nitrate is present, the tank is cycled. Seeding with mature filter media from an established tank can cut cycling time to 1-2 weeks.

## Tank Setup and Equipment

### Tank Size

Bigger tanks are more stable and forgiving of mistakes. A 75-liter (20-gallon) tank is the recommended minimum for beginners — small tanks experience rapid parameter swings. The classic stocking rule is roughly one centimeter of adult fish length per two liters of water, but this is a crude guideline. Active swimmers need horizontal swimming space; bottom dwellers need floor area; territorial fish need line-of-sight breaks.

### Filtration

The three types of filtration are:

- **Mechanical**: Sponge, filter floss, or foam traps particulate matter. Rinse in old tank water (not tap water, which contains chlorine that kills beneficial bacteria).
- **Biological**: Porous ceramic rings, bio-balls, or sponge provide surface area for nitrifying bacteria. This is the most critical filtration component.
- **Chemical**: Activated carbon removes dissolved organics, medications, and tannins. Not always necessary but useful for clarity and odor removal.

Hang-on-back (HOB) filters are convenient for small tanks. Canister filters provide superior biological and mechanical filtration for larger setups. Sponge filters powered by an air pump are ideal for shrimp tanks and fry tanks — gentle flow and no intake that sucks up tiny animals.

### Heating

Tropical fish require a heater to maintain 24-28°C (75-82°F). Use a reliable heater rated at 3-5 watts per liter. A stuck-on heater can cook the tank in hours — use an external temperature controller (Inkbird ITC-306T) as a safety cutoff, or keep two smaller heaters instead of one large one (if one fails, the other prevents catastrophic temperature drops).

## Water Parameters

Test water weekly with a liquid test kit. The critical parameters are:

- **Ammonia**: Must be 0 ppm at all times in a cycled tank.
- **Nitrite**: Must be 0 ppm at all times in a cycled tank.
- **Nitrate**: Keep below 20-40 ppm with regular water changes (25-50% weekly).
- **pH**: Most tropical fish adapt to a wide pH range (6.5-7.8). Stability matters more than hitting an exact number — don't chase a specific pH with chemicals.
- **GH (General Hardness)**: Measures calcium and magnesium. Soft-water fish (tetras, rasboras, discus) prefer 2-8 dGH; hard-water fish (livebearers, African cichlids) prefer 10-20 dGH.
- **KH (Carbonate Hardness)**: Buffering capacity. KH above 4 dKH prevents dangerous pH crashes. If your KH is very low, add crushed coral to the filter.

## Planted Tanks

Live plants consume nitrate, produce oxygen, provide hiding spots, and look beautiful. Low-tech plants (Java fern, Anubias, Java moss, Cryptocoryne) grow under standard aquarium lighting without CO2 injection. Attach Java fern and Anubias to hardscape (rocks, driftwood) — burying their rhizomes in substrate kills them.

High-tech planted tanks inject pressurized CO2 (typically 20-30 ppm, measured with a drop checker) and use high-intensity lighting (RGB LEDs or T5HO fluorescent). This drives rapid photosynthesis but demands regular fertilization — dose nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium (macro), and iron, manganese, boron (micro) according to the Estimative Index or PPS-Pro dosing method.

### Algae Control

Algae outbreaks indicate an imbalance between light, CO2, and nutrients. Too much light relative to CO2 and nutrients feeds algae instead of plants. Reduce the photoperiod to 6-8 hours, add floating plants to reduce light, and ensure CO2 and nutrients are adequate. Algae-eating crew members — Amano shrimp, Nerite snails, Otocinclus catfish, and Siamese algae eaters — help control but don't eliminate algae.

## Common Fish Diseases

- **Ich (white spot disease)**: Caused by the parasite Ichthyophthirius multifiliis. Presents as tiny white dots on fins and body. Treat by raising temperature to 30°C for 7-10 days and adding aquarium salt (1 tablespoon per 20 liters) or medication (malachite green, methylene blue).
- **Fin rot**: Bacterial infection causing frayed, disintegrating fins. Improve water quality first (ammonia and nitrite must be zero). Mild cases resolve with clean water alone; severe cases may need antibacterial treatment.
- **Dropsy**: Pinecone-like raised scales caused by internal organ failure or bacterial infection. Extremely difficult to treat — isolation and broad-spectrum antibiotics may help if caught early, but the prognosis is poor.
