# Cheese Making

## The Science of Cheese

Cheese making is the controlled spoilage of milk. The basic process hasn't changed in thousands of years: acidify milk, coagulate the proteins (casein) into a solid curd, separate the curd from the liquid whey, shape the curd, and age it. The variations in these steps — starter culture choice, coagulation method, cooking temperature, pressing pressure, salting technique, and aging conditions — produce the enormous diversity of cheese from creamy Brie to hard Parmigiano-Reggiano.

### Milk Selection

The quality of milk determines the quality of cheese. Use the freshest, highest-quality milk available. Raw (unpasteurized) milk produces the most complex flavors due to its native enzymes and bacterial flora, but carrying potential pathogens, it requires aging for at least 60 days in most jurisdictions. Pasteurized milk (heated to 72°C for 15 seconds) is safer but has diminished enzyme activity — add calcium chloride (1/4 teaspoon per gallon) to restore coagulation strength lost during pasteurization. Ultra-pasteurized (UHT) milk is heated to 135°C and cannot form a proper curd — never use it for cheese making.

Whole milk produces richer cheese. Goat's milk makes tangier, whiter cheese (it lacks carotene). Sheep's milk is highest in fat and protein, yielding more cheese per gallon and a rich, buttery flavor (Manchego, Roquefort, Pecorino).

## Starter Cultures

Starter cultures are specific strains of bacteria that convert lactose (milk sugar) into lactic acid, lowering the pH and developing flavor. Different cultures produce different characteristics:

- **Mesophilic cultures** (Lactococcus lactis, Leuconostoc): Operate at 20-40°C. Used for cheddar, Gouda, Brie, Camembert, blue cheese, and most soft cheeses.
- **Thermophilic cultures** (Streptococcus thermophilus, Lactobacillus helveticus): Operate at 40-55°C. Used for Italian-style cheeses — mozzarella, provolone, Parmesan, Swiss — where higher cooking temperatures are part of the process.
- **Surface-ripened cultures**: Penicillium candidum creates the white rind on Brie and Camembert. Brevibacterium linens produces the orange, pungent rind on washed-rind cheeses (Epoisses, Limburger).
- **Blue mold** (Penicillium roqueforti): Injected or mixed into the curd. Grows in air channels created by piercing the wheel with needles during aging, producing the characteristic blue-green veins and sharp, tangy flavor.

## Coagulation

### Rennet

Rennet is an enzyme (chymosin) that cleaves kappa-casein, causing milk proteins to aggregate into a gel-like curd. Traditional rennet comes from the stomach lining of young ruminants. Vegetable rennet (from the thistle plant Cynara cardunculus) and microbial rennet (fermentation-produced chymosin, often called FPC) are vegetarian alternatives. FPC is used in the majority of commercial cheese production worldwide.

Add rennet to warmed, acidified milk and stir gently for one minute. Then let the milk sit undisturbed for 30-60 minutes until a clean break forms — insert a finger or thermometer at an angle and lift; the curd should split cleanly with clear whey filling the gap. A weak, mushy curd means insufficient rennet or acidification.

### Acid Coagulation

Some fresh cheeses (ricotta, paneer, queso fresco) use acid alone — lemon juice, vinegar, or citric acid — to coagulate milk without rennet. Heat milk to 85-90°C, add acid, stir gently, and the curds form immediately. These cheeses are meant to be eaten fresh and don't age well because the curd structure is fragile.

## Curd Processing

After coagulation, cut the curd into cubes using a long knife or curd cutter. Smaller cuts (6mm) produce harder, drier cheese (Parmesan); larger cuts (15-20mm) retain more moisture for softer cheese (Brie, Camembert). Stir and cook the curds at controlled temperatures — heating to 38°C for cheddar, up to 52°C for Swiss and Parmesan — which expels more whey and firms the curd.

### Cheddaring

Cheddar cheese undergoes a unique process called cheddaring: the drained curd is formed into slabs, stacked, and flipped every 15 minutes for 1-2 hours. The weight of the stacking expels whey while the continued acid development by the starter culture produces a smooth, cohesive texture. The slabs are then milled (broken into small pieces), salted, pressed into molds at high pressure, and aged 3-24 months.

## Aging (Affinage)

Aging transforms fresh curd into complex, flavorful cheese through enzymatic and microbial activity. Aging caves or chambers maintain specific temperature (10-15°C) and humidity (80-95%) conditions. During aging:

- Proteins break down into peptides and amino acids, developing savory, umami flavors. The white crystals in aged Parmesan and Gouda are tyrosine amino acid clusters — a sign of quality aging.
- Fats oxidize and break down into fatty acids, contributing buttery and sharp notes.
- Surface molds and bacteria develop rind character — from the downy white Penicillium candidum on Brie to the orange Brevibacterium linens on Taleggio.

Turn wheels regularly (daily for the first week, then weekly) to ensure even moisture distribution and prevent sticking. Wash-rind cheeses are wiped with brine or spirit (beer, wine, brandy) during aging to encourage surface bacteria growth.

## Safety and Sanitation

Sanitize all equipment with a no-rinse sanitizer (Star San) or boiling water before use. Use a dedicated cheese thermometer — accuracy matters. Monitor pH with test strips or a pH meter at each stage. Keep aging cheese at proper temperature and humidity — too warm promotes spoilage bacteria; too dry causes cracking.
