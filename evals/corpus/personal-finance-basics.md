# Personal Finance Basics

## Budgeting

A budget is a plan for how to allocate your income. Without one, money tends to disappear into small, untracked expenses. The goal isn't restriction — it's awareness and intentional spending.

### The 50/30/20 Rule

A simple starting framework: allocate 50% of after-tax income to needs (rent, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments), 30% to wants (dining out, entertainment, subscriptions), and 20% to savings and extra debt repayment. Adjust the ratios based on your situation — someone with high-interest debt might flip to 50/20/30 until the debt is cleared.

### Zero-Based Budgeting

Every dollar gets a job. Income minus all planned expenses (including savings) should equal zero. This forces you to be deliberate about every category. Tools like YNAB (You Need A Budget) implement this approach.

### Tracking Expenses

Track every expense for at least one month before creating a budget. Use your bank's transaction export, a spreadsheet, or an app. Most people are surprised by how much goes to subscriptions and small recurring purchases they've forgotten about.

## Emergency Fund

Before investing or aggressively paying down debt, build an emergency fund of 3-6 months of essential expenses in a high-yield savings account. This prevents you from going into debt when unexpected expenses arise — car repairs, medical bills, job loss.

Start with a target of $1,000 as a "starter" emergency fund, then build to the full 3-6 months over time.

## Debt Management

### High-Interest Debt First (Avalanche Method)

List all debts by interest rate. Make minimum payments on everything, then throw every extra dollar at the highest-rate debt. Once it's paid off, redirect that payment to the next highest rate. This minimizes total interest paid.

### Smallest Balance First (Snowball Method)

List all debts by balance. Pay off the smallest balance first for a quick psychological win, then roll that payment into the next smallest. The snowball method costs more in interest than the avalanche method but keeps motivation high.

### Debt to Avoid

- **Credit card debt carried month to month**: Interest rates of 18-25% make this the most expensive consumer debt. Pay the full statement balance every month.
- **Payday loans**: Effective APRs often exceed 400%. Avoid at all costs.
- **Car loans on depreciating luxury vehicles**: Buy a reliable used car with cash or a short-term loan if needed.

## Investing Basics

### Start Early, Stay Consistent

The power of compound interest means starting early matters more than starting big. Investing $200/month from age 25 to 65 at a 7% average annual return yields roughly $525,000. Waiting until 35 to start with the same contribution yields only about $244,000 — less than half, despite only 10 fewer years.

### Index Funds

For most people, low-cost index funds that track the broad market (S&P 500, total stock market, total international) are the optimal investment. They provide instant diversification, have minimal fees (expense ratios under 0.10%), and historically outperform the majority of actively managed funds over long periods.

### Asset Allocation

Your mix of stocks and bonds should reflect your time horizon and risk tolerance. A common rule of thumb is "110 minus your age in stocks" — so a 30-year-old would hold 80% stocks and 20% bonds. This is a rough starting point; adjust based on your personal circumstances and comfort with volatility.

### Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Maximize contributions to tax-advantaged accounts before investing in taxable brokerage accounts:

1. **Employer 401(k) match**: Always contribute enough to get the full employer match — it's free money.
2. **Roth IRA or Traditional IRA**: Contribute up to the annual limit. Roth contributions are after-tax but grow and are withdrawn tax-free in retirement. Traditional contributions are tax-deductible now but taxed on withdrawal.
3. **HSA (Health Savings Account)**: If eligible, an HSA is triple tax-advantaged — contributions, growth, and qualified medical withdrawals are all tax-free.

## Insurance

Insurance is about protecting against catastrophic financial loss, not small inconveniences.

- **Health insurance**: Non-negotiable. A single hospital stay without insurance can result in tens of thousands of dollars of debt.
- **Renters/homeowners insurance**: Protects your possessions and provides liability coverage. Renters insurance typically costs $15-30/month.
- **Disability insurance**: Your ability to earn income is your most valuable asset. Long-term disability insurance replaces 50-70% of income if you can't work. Check if your employer provides it.
- **Term life insurance**: If others depend on your income (children, spouse), get a term policy covering 10-12x your annual income for 20-30 years. Skip whole life insurance — it's expensive and the investment component underperforms index funds.

## Financial Milestones by Age

| Age Range | Priorities |
|-----------|-----------|
| 20s | Build emergency fund, eliminate high-interest debt, start investing (even small amounts), establish credit history |
| 30s | Maximize retirement contributions, save for home down payment if desired, increase emergency fund, get disability insurance |
| 40s | Catch up on retirement if behind, begin college savings (529 plans), review insurance coverage, estate planning basics |
| 50s | Peak earning years — maximize retirement savings (catch-up contributions available), reduce investment risk gradually, plan healthcare for early retirement gap |
