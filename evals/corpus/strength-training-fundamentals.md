# Strength Training Fundamentals

## Why Strength Train

Strength training builds muscle mass, increases bone density, improves joint stability, and raises resting metabolic rate. It reduces the risk of injury in sports and daily life, combats age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia), and improves insulin sensitivity. It is equally important for runners, cyclists, and endurance athletes as it is for those focused purely on aesthetics or strength.

## Progressive Overload

The foundational principle of all strength training is progressive overload: gradually increasing the stress placed on the body over time. Without progressive overload, muscles have no reason to adapt. You can increase stress by adding weight to the bar, adding repetitions, adding sets, reducing rest time between sets, or improving the range of motion.

A common beginner progression is to add 2.5 kg to upper-body lifts and 5 kg to lower-body lifts each session until progress stalls. At that point, switch to weekly or bi-weekly progression, or use more advanced programming strategies.

## The Big Compound Movements

Compound exercises recruit multiple muscle groups across multiple joints and should form the core of any program:

- **Squat**: Works quads, glutes, hamstrings, and core. Back squats (bar on upper back) are the standard; front squats shift emphasis to quads and require more upper-back mobility.
- **Deadlift**: Works the entire posterior chain — hamstrings, glutes, lower and upper back, grip. Conventional stance has feet hip-width apart; sumo stance uses a wider stance with a narrower grip.
- **Bench press**: Primary chest exercise, also trains front delts and triceps. Control the bar down to the chest, pause briefly, then press up.
- **Overhead press**: Shoulders and triceps. Press a barbell from the front of the shoulders to full lockout overhead. Brace the core hard to avoid excessive lumbar extension.
- **Barbell row**: Upper back, lats, and biceps. Hinge at the hips, maintain a flat back, and pull the bar to the lower chest.

## Rep Ranges

Different rep ranges produce different adaptations:

| Reps | Primary Adaptation | Typical Rest |
|------|-------------------|-------------|
| 1-5 | Maximal strength, neural efficiency | 3-5 minutes |
| 6-12 | Hypertrophy (muscle growth) | 1-3 minutes |
| 12-20+ | Muscular endurance, metabolic stress | 30-90 seconds |

All rep ranges contribute to muscle growth, but the 6-12 range is most efficient for hypertrophy because it balances mechanical tension with sufficient volume. Most programs include work across multiple rep ranges.

## Programming

### Beginner (0-6 months)

Full-body workouts 3 days per week (e.g., Monday/Wednesday/Friday). Each session includes a squat variation, a press, a pull, and accessories. Linear progression — add weight every session.

### Intermediate (6-18 months)

Upper/lower split or push/pull/legs, training 4-6 days per week. Weekly or bi-weekly progression. Introduce periodization — structured cycles of volume and intensity. A simple block periodization scheme: 4 weeks of higher volume (3-4 sets of 8-12), then 4 weeks of higher intensity (4-5 sets of 3-6), then a deload week.

### Deload Weeks

Every 4-6 weeks, reduce training volume and/or intensity by 40-50% for one week. This allows accumulated fatigue to dissipate without losing adaptations. Signs you need a deload: persistent joint aches, stalled progress, poor sleep, low motivation.

## Warm-Up

Never skip the warm-up. Begin with 5-10 minutes of light cardio (rowing, cycling, jump rope) to raise core temperature. Then perform dynamic stretches targeting the muscles you'll train: leg swings, arm circles, hip circles, bodyweight squats. Finally, do 2-3 warm-up sets of your first exercise at progressively heavier weights before your working sets.

## Recovery

Muscles grow during recovery, not during the workout. Key recovery factors:

- **Sleep**: 7-9 hours per night. Growth hormone peaks during deep sleep.
- **Protein**: 1.6-2.2g per kg of body weight daily, spread across 3-5 meals. Post-workout protein timing matters less than total daily intake.
- **Rest between sessions**: Allow 48-72 hours before training the same muscle group again. This is why split routines exist — you train different muscle groups on different days.
- **Stress management**: Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which impairs recovery and promotes fat storage. Address sleep, workload, and mental health alongside training.

## Common Mistakes

- **Ego lifting**: Using weight too heavy to control through the full range of motion. This builds bad movement patterns and increases injury risk.
- **Neglecting posterior chain**: Bench press and curls are popular; rows and hamstring work are neglected. This creates imbalances that lead to shoulder and knee problems.
- **Program hopping**: Switching programs every few weeks prevents progressive overload from working. Commit to a program for at least 8-12 weeks before evaluating results.
- **Ignoring mobility**: If you can't squat to parallel depth or overhead press without flaring your ribs, address mobility limitations before adding weight.
