# Pottery and Ceramics

## Clay Types

Clay is the foundation of all ceramic work. The three main types used in studio pottery are:

### Earthenware

Earthenware fires at the lowest temperatures (900-1,150°C / cone 06-2). It remains porous after firing and must be glazed to hold liquid. Terra cotta is the most familiar earthenware — the classic reddish-orange flower pot. Earthenware is forgiving to work with and comes in red, brown, and white varieties. Its low firing temperature means lower kiln energy costs and compatibility with a wide range of colorful low-fire glazes.

### Stoneware

Stoneware fires at mid to high temperatures (1,200-1,300°C / cone 6-10) and vitrifies (becomes non-porous and glass-like) during firing. It's the most popular clay for functional ware — mugs, bowls, plates — because fired stoneware is durable, food-safe, and microwave/dishwasher-safe without requiring a glaze for waterproofing. Colors range from light buff to dark brown depending on iron content.

### Porcelain

Porcelain fires at the highest temperatures (1,260-1,400°C / cone 10-12) and becomes translucent when thin. It's the most refined clay — smooth, white, and elegant — but also the most demanding to work with. Porcelain has low plasticity (it cracks easily during forming), narrow drying tolerances, and extreme shrinkage (12-15% from wet to fired). Mastering porcelain takes years of experience.

## Wheel Throwing

Wheel throwing is the process of forming clay on a spinning potter's wheel. It produces symmetrical, round forms — bowls, cups, vases, plates.

### Centering

Centering is the foundational skill: forcing the clay into a perfectly symmetrical mound at the center of the spinning wheel. Anchor your elbows against your body or the wheel tray for stability. Use the heel of your palm to push the clay toward center while the wheel spins at medium-high speed. The clay is centered when it no longer wobbles and your hands remain still while touching it. Centering typically takes beginners weeks to learn consistently.

### Opening and Pulling

Once centered, press your thumbs into the center of the clay to create the opening (the inside of the vessel). Leave at least 6mm of clay at the bottom — thinner and the piece may crack during drying or collapse. Then pull the walls upward: squeeze the clay between your inside and outside fingers while slowly raising your hands. Each pull thins and heightens the wall. Three to five pulls typically achieve the desired height and thickness. A consistent wall thickness of 5-8mm is ideal for most functional ware.

### Trimming

After the thrown piece stiffens to leather-hard (firm but still slightly damp, typically 12-24 hours), flip it upside-down on the wheel and trim the foot ring — the recessed base that the piece sits on. A foot ring prevents the piece from rocking on uneven surfaces and provides a place to hold while glazing. Trim with a loop tool, removing excess clay from the bottom to achieve even wall thickness.

## Hand-Building Techniques

### Slab Building

Roll clay into flat slabs of uniform thickness (6-10mm) using a rolling pin and guide sticks. Cut shapes with a needle tool or template. Join slabs by scoring both surfaces with a fork, applying slip (liquid clay), pressing together, and smoothing the seam. Slab building produces angular, geometric forms — boxes, trays, tiles, architectural pieces.

### Coil Building

Roll clay into long coils (ropes) of consistent diameter and stack them to build walls. Blend the coils together on the inside for structural integrity; leave them visible on the outside for texture or smooth them for a clean surface. Coil building allows organic, asymmetrical forms of any size — from small cups to enormous garden pots.

## Glazing

Glaze is a glassy coating fused to the clay surface during firing. It provides color, texture, and a food-safe, waterproof surface. Glazes consist of three components: silica (glass former), flux (lowers melting point — feldspar, calcium, sodium, lithium), and alumina (prevents the glaze from running off the piece).

### Application Methods

- **Dipping**: Submerge the bisque-fired piece in a bucket of glaze for 1-3 seconds. Produces the most even coat.
- **Brushing**: Apply with a wide, soft brush in 2-3 even coats. Each coat should be applied perpendicular to the previous one.
- **Spraying**: Use an HVLP spray gun for even, controlled application. Best for large pieces or complex forms.
- **Pouring**: Pour glaze over the piece for dramatic drip effects.

Keep the foot ring free of glaze — glaze melts during firing and will permanently fuse the piece to the kiln shelf. Apply wax resist to the foot before glazing as insurance.

## Kiln Firing

Ceramic work is fired twice: bisque firing and glaze (glost) firing.

### Bisque Firing

The first firing (typically cone 06, ~1,000°C) transforms raw clay into a porous, durable state that can be handled and glazed without dissolving. Ramp the kiln slowly — especially through the quartz inversion at 573°C, where the crystalline structure of silica changes and the clay expands. Too-fast ramping causes cracking or explosion if moisture remains in the clay. Bone-dry pieces should be loaded loosely; they can touch each other during bisque firing.

### Glaze Firing

The second firing (cone 6 for stoneware, cone 10 for porcelain) melts the glaze and vitrifies the clay body. Pieces must not touch each other or the kiln shelves where glazed. Use kiln wash (a sacrificial coating of alumina and kaolin) on shelves to protect against drips. Cooling rate affects glaze surface — slow cooling through 1,100-1,000°C encourages crystal growth in some glazes, producing beautiful matte or variegated surfaces.

## Common Problems

- **S-cracks**: Cracks in the bottom of thrown pots. Caused by uneven compression during centering or uneven drying. Compress the bottom thoroughly after opening.
- **Crawling**: Glaze beads up and pulls away from the surface during firing, leaving bare patches. Usually caused by dust or oil on the bisque surface — clean bisque ware before glazing.
- **Bloating**: Large bubbles trapped in the clay wall that expand during firing. Caused by over-firing or organic material in the clay burning out too late in the firing cycle.
- **Dunting**: Cracks that form during cooling, often at the quartz inversion temperature. Cool the kiln slowly or leave the kiln closed until it reaches room temperature.
